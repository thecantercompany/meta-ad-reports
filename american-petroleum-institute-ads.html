Strategic Takeaway

API operates a coordinated two-page Meta strategy: the corporate "American Petroleum Institute" page targets the D.C. policy bubble with institutional messaging, while the "Energy Citizens" page — a documented API-funded advocacy brand since 2009 — runs high-volume grassroots mobilization in battleground and energy-producing states. Energy Citizens consistently outspends the corporate page by 10–20x during legislative battles, functioning as the constituent-pressure arm of a classic inside-outside lobbying operation. Combined, the two pages have spent $2M–$3.3M to deliver ~132 million impressions across 10,000+ ads, with spending spikes tightly correlated to specific legislative threats and policy opportunities.

Advertiser Profiles

Two Pages, One Strategy

Both pages are funded by the American Petroleum Institute — the largest U.S. oil and gas trade association, representing ~600 member corporations including ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell, BP, and ConocoPhillips. API reported $252 million in revenue in 2024. Its CEO, Mike Sommers, earned $5.1 million in compensation.

Astroturf Disclosure

Energy Citizens is not an independent organization. Launched in 2009, it operates as a campaign brand under API's umbrella. In August 2009, API president Jack Gerard sent an internal email (obtained by Greenpeace) urging member companies to provide "significant attendance" at Energy Citizens rallies, with "API providing the up-front resources." All Energy Citizens ads disclose "Paid for by AMERICAN PETROLEUM INSTITUTE" in the Ad Library. The PR firm Democracy Data & Communications — which received $43.2 million from API between 2011–2019 — hosted multiple websites designed to appear grassroots as part of the Energy Citizens infrastructure.

Campaign Chronicle

Five Years of API Advertising, Event by Event

1. The Biden Climate Plan Response (Jul–Aug 2020)

On July 14, 2020, Biden released his $2 trillion climate plan — committing to ban new oil and gas permits on public lands, achieve a carbon-free power sector by 2035, and reach net-zero by 2050. API responded immediately. The corporate page generated 3,189 ads in July alone, spending $54K–$375K — a defensive mobilization triggered by the single most consequential policy threat the industry had faced in years.

InfluenceMap documented a sharp uptick in oil and gas Facebook ads the day after Biden released the plan. API was part of a broader industry blitz: 25 oil and gas organizations collectively ran 25,147 ads on Facebook in 2020, viewed 431 million times, spending $9.6 million.

"America's energy leadership has delivered economic growth and environmental progress. But burdensome regulations and anti-energy policies could put our progress at risk." — API ad text, Jul–Sep 2020

2. The 2020 Election Battleground Blitz (Oct 2020)

In October 2020, API expanded its advertising into key battleground states including Michigan, Florida, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Arizona, Colorado, North Carolina, and Minnesota. Energy Citizens carried the heavy spending — $784K–$1M in a single month across 980 ads. The messaging framed energy policy as a ballot issue without naming candidates, a common issue-advocacy tactic to influence voter behavior without triggering FEC electioneering restrictions.

The swing-state targeting was strategic: Pennsylvania (fracking), Ohio (petrochemicals), Michigan (auto/energy), and Colorado (natural gas) all had active energy-economy debates. Energy Citizens' October 2020 spend was the largest single-month expenditure on either page at that point.

3. The Build Back Better War (Mar–Sep 2021)

Biden's American Jobs Plan (March 31, 2021) and the emerging Build Back Better reconciliation package triggered a sustained six-month advertising campaign. Energy Citizens maintained $91K–$729K in monthly spending from March through September, while the API corporate page ran targeted D.C. ads during key legislative moments.

The campaign reached peak intensity when the Senate passed the budget resolution in August 2021, unlocking the reconciliation path for the methane fee. API spent at least $423,000 on Facebook ads viewed 21 million times between August 11 and early October, setting a single-day spending record of $10,800 on August 8. Energy Citizens published 286 ads driving constituent calls against the reconciliation bill, gaining 6 million views.

API and Energy Citizens also launched a seven-figure TV and digital campaign specifically targeting the methane fee, with ads directing viewers to a site reading "Methane Tax: Devastating for Families and Businesses."

"The natural gas and oil industry is foundational to our nation's economic recovery and long-term growth, and federal lawmakers should ensure the industry can continue meeting this demand." — API ad text, Jul–Sep 2021

4. The Ukraine Energy Security Pivot (Feb–Apr 2022)

Russia's invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022 created a strategic opportunity for API. As crude oil surged past $100/barrel and gas prices climbed toward $5/gallon, API reframed the entire energy debate from "oil vs. climate" to "American energy security vs. foreign dependence." Energy Citizens spent $933K–$1.2M in April 2022 alone — 928 ads — the largest single-month spend since October 2020.

The messaging was explicit: "Would you rather get oil from America with strict environmental standards, or from hostile regimes that weaponize energy?" API CEO Sommers drew WWII parallels, positioning American oil workers as essential to allied security. In June, API released its "10-Point Policy Plan to Unleash American Energy and Fuel Recovery."

Industry Coordination

API's Ukraine pivot was not isolated. InfluenceMap documented that Energy Citizens ran 233 energy security ads viewed 19.6 million times in February–March 2022 — triple the rate of the previous quarter. The messaging was coordinated across the industry: multiple trade groups adopted the same "energy security" framing simultaneously, suggesting shared messaging strategy across API, the U.S. Chamber, and aligned groups.

5. The Inflation Reduction Act Response (Aug–Sep 2022)

The surprise Manchin-Schumer IRA deal on July 27, 2022 included the methane fee API had spent millions fighting. The law imposed a methane charge starting at $900/ton in 2024, rising to $1,500/ton by 2026, alongside $370 billion in clean energy tax credits. Energy Citizens spent $345K–$465K in August–September, pivoting from "stop the bill" to "punish supporters in the midterms."

API's response was strategically nuanced: Sommers welcomed provisions on mandatory lease sales and carbon capture while opposing the methane fee as an "energy tax during a crisis." The IRA was a partial defeat — the methane fee survived — but the mandatory lease sale provisions were a significant win that API had lobbied for.

6. The Regulatory Triple Threat (May–Oct 2023)

May 2023 saw the convergence of three major regulatory threats — the EPA power plant rule, the EPA tailpipe emissions rule, and the debt ceiling negotiations that included permitting reform. Energy Citizens responded with its largest single-month spend in the entire dataset: $1.4M–$1.7M in May 2023 across 198 ads, followed by $791K in June, $1.09M in September, and $1.32M in October.

This was a rare moment where API was simultaneously playing offense (pushing permitting reform into the debt ceiling deal) and defense (opposing EPA rules). The API corporate page ran ads with messaging like "U.S. natural gas and oil producers work hard to meet rising demand, but Washington's policies limit American energy." Energy Citizens mobilized constituents for over 12,000 actions on permitting reform, tailpipe emissions, and industry taxes during the congressional recess.

Cumulative Spending: May–Dec 2023

Energy Citizens spent an estimated $4.5M–$5.8M in the eight months from May through December 2023, making it the most expensive sustained campaign period in API's Meta advertising history. This was the high-water mark of the Biden administration's regulatory push against fossil fuels.

7. The "Lights On Energy" Election Blitz (Jan–Sep 2024)

API launched its eight-figure "Lights On Energy" campaign in January 2024 at the State of American Energy event, timed to the election cycle. The corporate page's March 2024 spend — $280K–$356K across 66 ads — was its largest since July 2020, driven by three simultaneous triggers: the Lights On Energy launch, Biden's January 26 LNG export pause, and EPA's finalization of strict tailpipe emissions standards on March 20.

The creative used a racecar metaphor: "America in a race for its future competing against foreign nations for energy leadership." Ads ran in Colorado, New Mexico, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and the D.C. market. In September, during the final election stretch, the corporate page spent $44K–$53K on CO2 pipeline advocacy and energy-as-ballot messaging, while Energy Citizens was notably quiet — the grassroots page had been inactive since late 2023, not resuming until October 2025.

"Let's celebrate the holidays with the power of American ingenuity — 43% of U.S. electricity comes from natural gas!" — API ad text, Dec 2024

8. The Permitting Reform Offensive (Sep 2025 – Present)

With a friendly administration in the White House, API pivoted from defensive spending to offensive advocacy. In September 2025, API launched its "When America Builds, America Wins" permitting reform campaign, releasing a policy roadmap alongside a seven-figure TV and digital buy. The corporate page spent $21K–$30K on initial ads, while Energy Citizens reactivated in October with $206K–$332K, ramping to its highest-ever single-month ad volume: 2,339 ads spending $577K–$915K in November 2025.

The ads supported the SPEED Act and the Republican reconciliation package, which included 30 mandatory offshore lease sales, permit-by-rule for oil and gas drilling, expedited LNG terminal permitting, and repeal of the IRA methane fee. The House passed the SPEED Act 221-196 on December 18, 2025.

"How many U.S. Presidents have called for American Energy dominance in the last 50 years? Every. Single. One." — API ad text, Sep 2025 – Feb 2026

Inside-Outside Strategy

The geographic split reveals a classic inside-outside lobbying model. The API corporate page's heavy D.C./Virginia/Maryland concentration shows a Beltway influence strategy — ads placed where lawmakers, staffers, and policy journalists live and scroll. Energy Citizens targets the districts where constituent pressure can influence votes: oil-producing states (TX, NM, LA, CO), Rust Belt battlegrounds (OH, MI, PA), and swing states (NC, GA, IA, MN). This geographic division maximizes pressure from both directions — policymakers see the institutional argument, their constituents deliver the political incentive.

Strategic Assessment

What This Data Reveals

API's Meta advertising operation is one of the most sophisticated issue-advocacy campaigns in the Ad Library — a purpose-built two-channel system designed to maximize pressure on policymakers while maintaining the appearance of grassroots support.

Key Findings

1. Spending is event-driven, not routine. API does not maintain steady-state advertising. Every major spending spike maps directly to a legislative, regulatory, or electoral trigger. This is a reactive mobilization model — resources surge when threats emerge and recede when the immediate crisis passes.

2. The two-page structure is functionally significant. The API corporate page and Energy Citizens are not redundant. They serve distinct strategic functions: the corporate page delivers institutional credibility to D.C. insiders, while Energy Citizens generates constituent volume in target districts. The geographic and demographic splits confirm this is by design, not accident.

3. 2023 was the peak year. The convergence of EPA's power plant rule, tailpipe emissions rule, and methane rule finalization — combined with permitting reform negotiations — drove Energy Citizens' estimated $4.5M–$5.8M spend from May through December 2023. This was the Biden administration's regulatory high-water mark, and API's spending reflected the industry's assessment of existential threat.

4. The 2025 pivot is strategic, not just tactical. API's shift from defensive spending (fighting regulations) to offensive spending (pushing permitting reform) after the 2024 election represents a fundamental change in posture. The November 2025 Energy Citizens spike — 2,339 ads, the highest single-month volume ever — is not a defensive surge. It's a strategic mobilization to lock in legislative gains while the political window is open.

5. The "Demand Decade" is the new master narrative. API's branding of 2025–2035 as the "Demand Decade" — projecting 35–50% growth in U.S. electricity demand from AI, data centers, and electrification — provides the overarching frame for all current messaging. Permitting reform, LNG exports, and anti-EV regulation are all justified within this narrative as necessary to meet surging demand.

What the Ad Library Doesn't Show

Meta's Ad Library captures only one channel of API's advocacy spending. API spent $750 million on PR and advertising between 2008–2019. Its 2024 "Lights On Energy" campaign was described as an "eight-figure" buy spanning TV, streaming, and digital. The Meta spend documented here — $2M–$3.3M — represents the visible tip of a much larger advertising iceberg. API also spent $6.25 million on federal lobbying in 2024 alone, with its PAC steering $2.5 million to the Senate Leadership Fund.

Methodology

Data Sources & Limitations

This report analyzes political and issue advertising data from Meta's Ad Library for two Facebook Pages operated by the American Petroleum Institute: "The American Petroleum Institute" (Page ID: 67945990059) and "Energy Citizens" (Page ID: 113891694102). Data was retrieved on February 10, 2026.

Search parameters: All ads reaching the United States, no date restriction (full history). Both pages returned 5,000 ads each (the Ad Library API maximum per query), meaning the true total ad count exceeds 10,000.

Spend figures: Meta reports spending in ranges (e.g., "$100–$199"), not exact amounts. All figures in this report use either the lower bound, upper bound, or midpoint as noted. Combined totals are approximations.

Demographics and geography: All demographic and geographic data reflects ad delivery results — who saw the ads — not targeting inputs. Delivery skews can be caused by first-party data, lookalike audiences, algorithmic optimization, creative resonance, or platform effects, in addition to explicit targeting choices.

Ad text: Some older ads (particularly Energy Citizens ads from 2021–2023) returned "No text" in the Ad Library, likely due to video or image-only creative formats. Messaging analysis is based on ads with available text content.

Known limitations: The 5,000-ad API cap means this report captures a subset of total advertising activity. Monthly spend figures from the Ad Library's time-series endpoint may differ slightly from page-level summaries due to how Meta attributes spend across ad delivery periods. Background research was conducted using public sources including API press releases, news coverage, InfluenceMap reports, OpenSecrets data, and ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer filings.
