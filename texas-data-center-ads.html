The Landscape
Texas Is Ground Zero for America's Data Center Boom
Texas is poised to overtake Northern Virginia as the nation's top data center market. ERCOT projects 70.5 GW of new load requests by 2028, primarily from data centers. The state saw 58 GW of new gas power projects announced in 2025 alone—more capacity than California's entire peak demand. Amid this explosive growth, communities are pushing back over water consumption, grid strain, emissions, and housing displacement, while the Texas Legislature enacted Senate Bill 6 (the "Kill Switch Bill") requiring data centers to pay their own interconnection costs and submit to emergency disconnection.
Key Events Timeline
June 2025
Senate Bill 6 signed into law. Requires data centers over 75 MW to pay interconnection costs, enables ERCOT to disconnect them during emergencies, and mandates backup generation. Industry calls it the "Kill Switch Bill."
August 2025
Fermi America files air permits for 6 GW of gas power at Project Matador near Amarillo. Community opposition begins to organize.
September 2025
Stargate opens first data center in Abilene. OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank announce five more facilities. Abilene begins experiencing a housing crisis as rents double.
October 2025
Chevron announces first-ever power plant for AI data centers in West Texas—2.5 GW initial phase. VoltaGrid signs 2.3 GW deal with Oracle.
November 2025
Google announces $40B Texas investment—three new data centers in West Texas and the Panhandle. College Station unanimously rejects a 600 MW data center proposal.
December 2025
Project Matador public hearing draws 279+ comments, mostly in opposition. Sierra Club labels the project a pollution threat. $1.4B in new Texas data center filings in December alone.
January 2026
ERCOT considers revisiting approved data center grid connections. Bloomberg reports the grid operator may need to re-evaluate projects given the magnitude of load growth.
February 2026
GW Ranch receives the nation's largest air pollution permit—7.65 GW of gas-fired power in the Permian Basin, authorized to emit 33 million tons/year of greenhouse gases (5% of Canada's total).

Special Section
Powering the Boom: Energy Generation for Texas Data Centers
Data centers are not just consuming electricity—they are building their own power plants at a scale that dwarfs most utility companies. Texas is the epicenter of this trend, driven by ERCOT's deregulated market, abundant natural gas, and Senate Bill 6's requirement that large loads "pay their own way." The result is a parallel energy infrastructure emerging outside the traditional grid.

Ad Landscape Gap
Despite $100B+ in announced energy infrastructure spending in Texas for data centers, the political advertising footprint on Meta for these projects is virtually nonexistent. No energy company—Pacifico, Fermi, Chevron, VoltaGrid, Crusoe, or Lancium—is running issue ads on Meta to build community support or manage public perception. The only ad directly referencing Texas energy-for-data-centers came from the Environmental Integrity Project (Oil and Gas Watch), an opposition watchdog. This advertising vacuum is a strategic vulnerability: opposition groups and media coverage are defining the narrative around these projects without organized industry rebuttal on social platforms.

Strategic Takeaway

Texas voters are hearing one message on data centers: the fossil fuel industry is here to help.

The verified Texas political ad landscape is dominated by pro-energy voices. Jim Wright (Railroad Commission Chairman, $46K–$67K) emphasizes Texas energy jobs and pro-industry regulation. TXOGA ($39K–$62K) explicitly positions natural gas as the fuel for data centers: "Texas data centers demand massive energy. Discover why natural gas is key to fueling them." Local candidates like Shelby Williams (Collin County) call for grid infrastructure to meet "massive growth in Texas."

Opposition is minimal. Ben Bius (TX-10 congressional candidate, $9K–$12K) is the only voice running anti-data-center ads, warning of "Californication" and tech billionaires taking Texas water and straining the grid. No environmental group is running Texas-targeted campaigns on this issue.

The hyperscalers are completely silent. Despite $500B+ in announced data center investment in Texas—Stargate in Abilene, Project Matador in Amarillo, Meta in El Paso, the GW Ranch complex in the Permian—not a single tech company is running political issue ads in Texas. No Oracle, no Microsoft, no OpenAI, no Google, no Meta. The companies actually building the infrastructure have ceded the narrative to the fossil fuel industry and local politicians.

The implication: Texas voters forming opinions about data centers are seeing messaging that frames the buildout as an opportunity for the oil and gas industry, not as a threat to water, grid, or communities. The absence of environmental opposition advertising and tech company engagement leaves TXOGA's "natural gas powers the digital economy" framing largely uncontested.

Methodology & Limitations

Data source: Meta Ad Library API. All ads classified as political, social, or issue advertising on Facebook, Instagram, Messenger, and Audience Network.

Date range: August 1, 2025 – February 4, 2026.

Scope: This report focuses exclusively on ads verified as running in Texas. Advertisers were verified via: (1) delivery data showing Texas as the primary target state, or (2) the advertiser being a Texas political candidate, Texas state agency, or Texas-based organization with Texas-specific content.

Search parameters: Keywords included "Texas data center," "Texas natural gas energy," "ERCOT," "Texas grid," and related terms. Page-level analysis conducted for all advertisers with Texas-relevant activity.

Limitations: Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact values. The API's regional delivery data shows the top state for each ad but does not provide precise percentages. National campaigns that mention Texas in their content but deliver nationally are excluded from this report. This analysis covers Meta platforms only; advertisers may be running parallel campaigns on Google, YouTube, programmatic display, direct mail, or broadcast media not captured here.
