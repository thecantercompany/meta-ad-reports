Executive Summary

Higher education has become a major political battleground, with over $2 million spent on Meta political ads in the past year. The dominant narrative is being shaped by conservative institutions and advocacy groups, who outspend progressive voices by roughly 3-to-1 on campus-focused messaging‚Äîwhile teachers' unions lead overall education spending with a focus on K-12 issues that increasingly intersect with higher ed policy.

This intelligence brief analyzes political advertising from higher education institutions, faculty unions, advocacy groups, and think tanks on Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram). The landscape reveals a fragmented ecosystem where traditional universities are largely absent from political advertising, ceding the narrative space to ideological advocacy organizations on both sides‚Äîthough conservative voices hold a commanding lead in spend and volume.

Strategic Takeaway

The absence of mainstream universities from political advertising is itself a strategic position‚Äîone that leaves the public narrative about higher education to be defined by ideological combatants. Hillsdale College, a small private institution in Michigan, has spent more on Meta political ads than all public university systems combined. This asymmetry means that when Americans encounter political messaging about colleges on social media, they're overwhelmingly seeing conservative critiques rather than institutional defenses.

The teachers' unions (NEA, AFT) represent the most significant progressive counterweight, but their messaging focuses primarily on K-12 educator pay and working conditions‚Äînot on defending higher education institutions themselves. This creates a narrative vacuum that conservative organizations have effectively filled with messaging about DEI, campus protests, and ideological "indoctrination."

What's Missing: The Silent Majority

Perhaps more revealing than who's advertising is who isn't. The following categories of institutions are largely absent from political advertising on Meta:

Public University Systems

The University of California, SUNY, University of Texas system, and other major public universities have minimal political ad presence. Their occasional ads focus on labor issues or institutional announcements‚Äînot on defending higher education's value or countering political attacks.

Elite Private Universities

Harvard, Yale, Columbia, Stanford (beyond Hoover) and other elite institutions are absent from political advertising despite being frequent targets of political criticism. They've chosen "institutional neutrality" over engagement.

For-Profit Colleges

University of Phoenix, DeVry, Grand Canyon University and other for-profits have essentially no political ad presence on Meta‚Äîdespite being subjects of political controversy around student loan policies and predatory practices.

Higher Ed Association Groups

The American Council on Education, Association of American Universities, and similar groups representing institutional interests are not advertising‚Äîleaving individual advocacy groups to shape public perception.

Policy Context

The advertising landscape reflects a broader political assault on higher education institutions at both federal and state levels.

Federal Level (2025)

  • Trump Executive Orders 14151 and 14173 dismantled federal DEI programs and mandates
  • Department of Education ruled race-based scholarships and cultural centers violate civil rights law
  • Threats to revoke federal funding from non-compliant institutions

State Level (2024-2025)

  • Florida, Texas, North Carolina: DEI program bans enacted
  • Ohio (SB1), Kentucky: Tenure protections weakened; DEI offices prohibited
  • Texas (HB 1830): Proposed bill to eliminate tenure entirely at public institutions
  • 26 states: Introduced bills censoring higher education in 2025

The Ad Response

Do No Harm Medicine's advertising spiked immediately following Trump's January 2025 executive orders, with "victory" messaging celebrating DEI's dismantlement. By contrast, no major institution or progressive advocacy group mounted a significant ad response defending DEI programs.

Methodology & Limitations

This report analyzes political and issue ads from Meta's Ad Library API for the period February 4, 2024 through February 4, 2025. Data was retrieved on February 4, 2025.

  • Search terms: "university," "college," "higher education," "campus," "DEI," "academic freedom," "faculty," "school choice," and related terms
  • Geography: United States only
  • Spend figures: Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact values. Upper bounds are used for rankings.
  • Impression figures: Similarly reported as ranges
  • Coverage: Only includes ads classified by Meta as political/social issue ads. Institutional marketing and enrollment ads are excluded unless they triggered Meta's political ad classification.
  • Demographic data: Reflects who saw ads (reach), not who was targeted. Algorithmic delivery may differ from advertiser intent.
