Executive Summary

Conservative institutions and advocacy groups dominate political advertising about higher education on Meta platforms, outspending all other voices combined. Traditional universities are almost entirely absent from this space, leaving the public narrative about colleges to be defined by ideological combatants‚Äîprimarily critics.

This intelligence brief analyzes political advertising from higher education institutions, faculty/staff unions, advocacy groups, and think tanks on Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram). The landscape reveals a lopsided ecosystem where Hillsdale College‚Äîa small private institution with 1,600 students‚Äîspends more on political ads than all public university systems combined. The Hoover Institution, operating from Stanford's campus, runs a sophisticated multi-platform content operation that reaches nearly 20 million impressions annually. Faculty unions have minimal presence, and progressive voices defending higher education are largely absent.

Strategic Takeaway

The absence of mainstream universities from political advertising is itself a strategic position‚Äîone that leaves the public narrative about higher education to be defined by critics. Hillsdale College, the Hoover Institution, and Do No Harm Medicine collectively spend more than 50x what faculty unions and progressive defenders of higher education invest in Meta advertising.

When Americans encounter political messaging about colleges on social media, they overwhelmingly see conservative critiques of DEI, "woke ideology," and campus culture‚Äînot institutional defenses or progressive counterarguments. Faculty unions like UPTE-CWA (University of California) focus narrowly on labor disputes rather than broader advocacy, leaving a massive narrative vacuum that conservative organizations have filled.

What's Missing: The Silent Majority

Perhaps more revealing than who's advertising is who isn't. Major categories of higher education stakeholders are completely absent from political advertising on Meta:

Public University Systems

The University of California, SUNY, University of Texas system, and other major public universities have minimal political ad presence. The UC system spent only $6K-$14K‚Äîless than 3% of what Hillsdale College spent. Their occasional ads focus on labor issues or institutional announcements‚Äînot on defending higher education's value or responding to political attacks.

Elite Private Universities

Harvard, Yale, Columbia, MIT, and other elite institutions are absent from political advertising despite being frequent targets of political criticism over DEI, legacy admissions, and campus protests. They've chosen "institutional neutrality" over engagement in the political advertising space.

AAUP & Faculty Associations

The American Association of University Professors‚Äîthe primary national organization representing faculty and defending academic freedom‚Äîhas zero political advertising presence on Meta. As state legislatures pass laws restricting tenure and academic freedom, AAUP is not present in the social media conversation.

Higher Ed Association Groups

The American Council on Education, Association of American Universities, Association of Public and Land-grant Universities, and similar groups representing institutional interests are not advertising‚Äîleaving advocacy groups to shape public perception without organized counterweight.

Policy Context

The advertising landscape reflects a broader political offensive against higher education institutions at both federal and state levels‚Äîan offensive that has accelerated significantly in 2024-2025.

Federal Level (2025)

  • Trump Executive Orders 14151 and 14173 dismantled federal DEI programs and mandates
  • Department of Education ruled race-based scholarships and cultural centers violate civil rights law
  • Threats to revoke federal funding from institutions with DEI programs
  • Congressional hearings targeting university presidents over antisemitism

State Level (2024-2025)

  • Florida, Texas, North Carolina: DEI program bans enacted and enforced
  • Ohio (SB1), Kentucky: Tenure protections weakened; DEI offices prohibited
  • Texas (HB 1830): Proposed bill to eliminate tenure entirely at public institutions
  • North Dakota (HB 1437): Faculty limited to 50% of tenure review committees
  • 26 states: Introduced bills censoring higher education in 2025

The Ad Response Gap

Do No Harm Medicine's advertising spiked immediately following Trump's January 2025 executive orders, with "victory" messaging celebrating DEI's dismantlement. By contrast, no major institution, faculty organization, or progressive advocacy group mounted a significant ad response defending DEI programs or academic freedom. The policy debate is being waged in state legislatures and federal agencies‚Äîbut only one side is showing up in the social media advertising space.

Methodology & Limitations

This report analyzes political and issue ads from Meta's Ad Library API for the period February 4, 2024 through February 4, 2025. Data was retrieved on February 4, 2025.

  • Search terms: "university," "college," "higher education," "campus," "DEI," "academic freedom," "faculty," "professor," "tenure," and related terms
  • Exclusions: K-12 education, school voucher/choice campaigns; teachers unions included only where messaging specifically addresses higher education issues
  • Geography: United States only
  • Spend figures: Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact values. Upper bounds are used for rankings.
  • Impression figures: Similarly reported as ranges
  • Coverage: Only includes ads classified by Meta as political/social issue ads. Institutional marketing and enrollment ads are excluded unless they triggered Meta's political ad classification.
  • Demographic data: Reflects who saw ads (reach), not who was targeted. Algorithmic delivery may differ from advertiser intent.
