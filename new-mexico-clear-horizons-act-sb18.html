Strategic Takeaway

The oil and gas industry deployed a Meta advertising blitz against SB 18 that outspent supporters by roughly 7-to-1 ‚Äî and the bill died on the Senate floor 19-23 on February 11, one day before its scheduled calendar date, when seven Democratic senators broke ranks. NMOGA alone accounted for 76% of all spending on this issue. Two additional opposition advertisers ‚Äî Consumer Energy Alliance (an industry front group) and New Mexico Rural Electric Cooperatives ‚Äî entered the fight in the final days, bringing total opposition advertisers to four. Despite clearing two committees for the first time in the bill's seven-year history, the support side's late surge of legislator-targeted ads was not enough to overcome the spending gap. The bill's defeat leaves New Mexico's emissions reduction framework resting entirely on an executive order that expires with Governor Lujan Grisham in January 2027.

Background

What Is the Clear Horizons Act?

Senate Bill 18 would codify Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham's 2019 executive order on greenhouse gas emission reduction targets into state law: 45% by 2030, 75% by 2040, and net-zero by 2050, measured against 2005 baselines. The bill expands the Environmental Improvement Board's authority to regulate large emitters (10,000+ metric tons per year), sets methane intensity standards for oil and gas production, and requires annual emissions inventories.

This matters now because Lujan Grisham is term-limited and leaves office in January 2027. Without codification, her successor could reverse the targets with a stroke of a pen. The bill has failed four times since 2019 ‚Äî most recently in 2025 as SB 4, when it was tabled in the Senate Finance Committee 6-5 after two Democrats voted with Republicans.

Legislative Timeline

The 2026 session was a 30-day session (budget-focused) that opened January 20 and ended February 19. SB 18 was introduced on January 23 and cleared two Senate committees ‚Äî a first for the Clear Horizons Act. The bill reached the Senate floor on February 11 and was defeated 19-23 after roughly one hour of debate. Seven Democratic senators voted with all Republican members to kill the bill. It never reached the House.

The Revenue Dependency Context

Oil and gas generates approximately $2.6 billion annually for New Mexico ‚Äî roughly 41% of all state operating revenue. This dependency is the central tension underlying SB 18. NMOGA claims the bill could cut $740 million to $2.2 billion per year in state and local revenue. Supporters counter that climate inaction would cost the state an estimated $294 billion by 2050.

Outcome

SB 18 Defeated 19-23

The bill died on the Senate floor on February 11 after roughly one hour of debate. It never reached the House.

Seven Democratic senators broke ranks and voted with all Republican members to kill the bill. The defectors represent oil-producing districts, rural communities, and tribal areas ‚Äî constituencies where NMOGA's economic messaging landed hardest.

Without codification, New Mexico's emissions reduction framework rests entirely on Governor Lujan Grisham's 2019 executive order ‚Äî which her successor can rescind with a stroke of a pen. The 2027 gubernatorial race will effectively become a referendum on whether the state's climate policies continue, making this a topic likely to generate continued political advertising through 2026 and into 2027.

Ad Activity Over Time

NMOGA's Spending Surge

NMOGA's ad spending followed a clear pattern: pre-session positioning followed by aggressive escalation as the bill advanced through committees ‚Äî then abrupt silence. The week the session opened (Jan 19), NMOGA spent more than $34,000 ‚Äî roughly triple any prior week. But after the Tax, Business & Transportation Committee passed the bill on Feb 8, NMOGA's Meta advertising effectively stopped. No new ads were launched after Feb 9, and the one remaining ad from that date spent under $99. In retrospect, NMOGA's pivot away from Meta ads after Feb 8 appears to have been a strategic shift to direct lobbying for the floor vote. The bill was defeated 19-23 just three days later, with seven Democrats voting no ‚Äî including senators from oil-producing and rural districts where NMOGA's earlier saturation campaign had been heaviest.

NMOGA began advertising on October 30, 2025 ‚Äî nearly three months before the session opened and two months before SB 18 was formally introduced. This pre-positioning ‚Äî combined with NMOGA's pivot from Meta ads to direct lobbying in the final days ‚Äî proved to be an effective two-phase strategy: build public opposition, then close with personal legislative persuasion.

Strategic Assessment

Two Different Playbooks

The opposition and support campaigns deployed fundamentally different advertising strategies. With the bill now defeated, the opposition's approach proved more effective ‚Äî though how much the Meta campaigns themselves influenced the vote versus direct lobbying is unknowable.

Opposition Strategy: Saturation

  • Objective: Shape public opinion broadly to pressure all legislators
  • Budget: ~$100K on Meta alone (likely 6-figures+ across all channels)
  • Targeting: All of New Mexico, broad demographics
  • Volume: 264 ads across 4 Pages, multiple creative variants, continuous presence
  • Cross-platform: TV ads, website action tools, legislative testimony, grassroots outreach

Support Strategy: Precision

  • Objective: Persuade specific swing legislators through constituent pressure
  • Budget: ~$14K on Meta (supplemented by organizing, rallies, testimony)
  • Targeting: Named individual senators and House members, committee-timed ads
  • Volume: 73 ads, concentrated around hearing dates and floor vote
  • Cross-platform: Coalition website, Climate Solutions Day rally, 41+ org sign-on letters

The support side's precision approach had an advantage NMOGA's saturation campaign could not replicate: specificity. An ad saying "Call Senator Cervantes today" creates direct, attributable constituent pressure on a named legislator. But the results suggest this specificity was insufficient ‚Äî five of the eight senators named in ProgressNow's targeted ads voted against the bill anyway.

The opposition's spending advantage created an asymmetric information environment throughout the campaign. A New Mexico Facebook or Instagram user encountered roughly 12 opposition impressions for every 1 support impression. Over the 94-day window, opposition advertisers served an estimated 9.8 to 11.5 million impressions in a state of 2.1 million people ‚Äî equivalent to roughly 5 ad impressions per person. Consumer Energy Alliance's late entry was particularly efficient, generating 589K‚Äì732K impressions on just $1,500‚Äì$3,282 in spend ‚Äî suggesting either highly optimized targeting or a pre-existing New Mexico audience.

Beyond Meta

Cross-Platform Context

Meta is one channel. The advertising battle over SB 18 extends well beyond Facebook and Instagram.

NMOGA & Clear Frontier New Mexico

NMOGA operated a dedicated opposition page at nmoga.org/sb_18_clear_horizons_act with an action tool for constituents to contact legislators. Separately, Clear Frontier New Mexico (a Farmington-based group) spent approximately $55,000 on television ads across four network stations opposing SB 18 ‚Äî making TV a larger opposition investment than any single Meta advertiser. NMOGA cited electricity rate increases (9‚Äì10 cents/kWh in 2019 to ~16 cents by 2025, a 50% jump) and a lack of cost caps or affordability protections in SB 18 during floor debate.

Americans for Prosperity

AFP-NM described their SB 18 opposition as a "grassroots and digital campaign" including voter contact efforts (phone, door-to-door, text), legislative testimony, and digital ads. Nationally, AFP launched a seven-figure "Road to Prosperity" campaign on energy deregulation, of which the NM effort was a state-level component. AFP state director Sarah Hetemi testified against SB 18 before the Senate Conservation Committee.

Clear Horizons Coalition

The 41+ organization coalition operated clearhorizonsnm.org with press releases, fact sheets, and advocacy tools. On-the-ground activities included a Climate Solutions Day rally in the Rotunda on February 5 (students from Acequia Madre Elementary and Rio Grande Charter School wore masks depicting New Mexican species) and organized public testimony across both committee hearings. The coalition also spent approximately $94,000 on television ads in support of SB 18 ‚Äî significantly more than their combined Meta spend of ~$14K.

Data Limitation

This report covers only Meta (Facebook/Instagram) advertising. Total advertising across all channels was significantly higher: opposition TV spending alone (~$55K from Clear Frontier NM) exceeded any single Meta advertiser except NMOGA, and the support coalition spent ~$94K on TV ‚Äî more than 6x their entire Meta spend. Full lobbying and advertising disclosure reports are due in May 2026. Meta spending should be read as a floor, not a ceiling, of total advertising activity.
