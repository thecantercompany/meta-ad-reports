Strategic Takeaway

The oil and gas industry has deployed a Meta advertising blitz against SB 18 that outspends supporters by a ratio of roughly 17-to-1. The New Mexico Oil & Gas Association alone accounts for 82% of all spending on this issue, spending more in two days (Feb 7‚Äì8) than all four pro-bill advertisers spent in their entire campaigns combined. Despite the spending imbalance, the bill has advanced further than any prior version of the Clear Horizons Act ‚Äî clearing two Senate committees for the first time in seven years of attempts.

Background

What Is the Clear Horizons Act?

Senate Bill 18 would codify Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham's 2019 executive order on greenhouse gas emission reduction targets into state law: 45% by 2030, 75% by 2040, and net-zero by 2050, measured against 2005 baselines. The bill expands the Environmental Improvement Board's authority to regulate large emitters (10,000+ metric tons per year), sets methane intensity standards for oil and gas production, and requires annual emissions inventories.

This matters now because Lujan Grisham is term-limited and leaves office in January 2027. Without codification, her successor could reverse the targets with a stroke of a pen. The bill has failed four times since 2019 ‚Äî most recently in 2025 as SB 4, when it was tabled in the Senate Finance Committee 6-5 after two Democrats voted with Republicans.

Legislative Timeline

The 2026 session is a 30-day session (budget-focused) that opened January 20 and ends February 19. SB 18 was introduced on January 23 and has cleared two committees ‚Äî a first for the Clear Horizons Act. It now needs a Senate floor vote, House committees, and a House floor vote, all within roughly 10 days.

The Revenue Dependency Context

Oil and gas generates approximately $2.6 billion annually for New Mexico ‚Äî roughly 41% of all state operating revenue. This dependency is the central tension underlying SB 18. NMOGA claims the bill could cut $740 million to $2.2 billion per year in state and local revenue. Supporters counter that climate inaction would cost the state an estimated $294 billion by 2050.

Note on Pre-Session Positioning

NMOGA began advertising on October 30, 2025 ‚Äî nearly three months before the session opened and two months before SB 18 was formally introduced. The earliest ads used "softer" messaging ("Our energy producers are cutting methane emissions... where are your priorities?") designed to shape the narrative before the bill became public. This pre-positioning is consistent with an industry-standard advocacy strategy of building opposition sentiment before a legislative threat materializes.

Delivery & Geographic Focus

NMOGA's ads were delivered overwhelmingly within New Mexico (estimated 100% of delivery), with trace spillover into neighboring states Texas, Arizona, Colorado, and Oklahoma ‚Äî likely from users with cross-state connections rather than intentional geographic targeting. The ads ran on both Facebook and Instagram across all 174 creatives, suggesting a broad-reach strategy aimed at saturating the New Mexico electorate.

Strategic Assessment

Two Different Playbooks

The opposition and support campaigns represent fundamentally different advertising strategies ‚Äî and both reflect rational choices given their resources.

Opposition Strategy: Saturation

  • Objective: Shape public opinion broadly to pressure all legislators
  • Budget: ~$94K on Meta alone (likely 6-figures+ across all channels)
  • Targeting: All of New Mexico, broad demographics
  • Volume: 244 ads, multiple creative variants, continuous presence
  • Cross-platform: TV ads, website action tools, legislative testimony, grassroots outreach

Support Strategy: Precision

  • Objective: Persuade specific swing legislators through constituent pressure
  • Budget: ~$12K on Meta (supplemented by organizing, rallies, testimony)
  • Targeting: Named individual senators, committee-timed ads
  • Volume: 46 ads, concentrated around hearing dates
  • Cross-platform: Coalition website, Climate Solutions Day rally, 41+ org sign-on letters

The support side's precision approach has an advantage NMOGA's saturation campaign cannot replicate: specificity. An ad saying "Call Senator Stefanics today" creates direct, attributable constituent pressure on a named legislator. NMOGA's ads shape the general environment, but cannot generate the same kind of targeted heat on individual decision-makers.

That said, NMOGA's spending advantage creates an asymmetric information environment. A New Mexico Facebook or Instagram user encountered roughly 40 opposition impressions for every 1 support impression. Over the 90-day window, NMOGA alone served 8.8 to 10.3 million impressions in a state of 2.1 million people ‚Äî equivalent to roughly 4 to 5 ad impressions per person.

Beyond Meta

Cross-Platform Context

Meta is one channel. The advertising battle over SB 18 extends well beyond Facebook and Instagram.

NMOGA

NMOGA operates a dedicated opposition page at nmoga.org/sb_18_clear_horizons_act with an action tool for constituents to contact legislators, plus a separate economic impact page. In prior sessions, NMOGA ran television ad campaigns against the Clear Horizons Act. Given the scale of their 2026 Meta spend, TV and digital video campaigns are likely running concurrently.

Americans for Prosperity

AFP-NM described their SB 18 opposition as a "grassroots and digital campaign" including voter contact efforts (phone, door-to-door, text), legislative testimony, and digital ads. Nationally, AFP launched a seven-figure "Road to Prosperity" campaign on energy deregulation, of which the NM effort is a state-level component.

Clear Horizons Coalition

The 41+ organization coalition operates clearhorizonsnm.org with press releases, fact sheets, and advocacy tools. On-the-ground activities include a Climate Solutions Day rally in the Rotunda on February 5 (students from Acequia Madre Elementary and Rio Grande Charter School wore masks depicting New Mexican species) and organized public testimony across both committee hearings.

Data Limitation

This report covers only Meta (Facebook/Instagram) advertising. NMOGA's total advertising budget across all channels ‚Äî including TV, Google/YouTube, programmatic display, direct mail, and radio ‚Äî is likely significantly higher than the $64K‚Äì$87K tracked here. The same applies to AFP's national "Road to Prosperity" campaign. Meta spending should be read as a floor, not a ceiling, of total advertising activity.

Data Sources & Limitations

This report draws from two data sources:

Meta Ad Library API ‚Äî All political and issue ad data was retrieved via the Meta Ad Library API on February 9, 2026. Search parameters included:

  • Page-based searches for all six identified advertisers
  • Keyword searches: "Clear Horizons Act," "SB 18 New Mexico," "New Mexico emissions oil gas," "New Mexico climate energy clean"
  • Date range: November 11, 2025 ‚Äì February 9, 2026

Public reporting and web sources ‚Äî Legislative context, organization profiles, and cross-platform advertising activity were gathered from news reporting by Source New Mexico, Albuquerque Journal, KRQE, Santa Fe New Mexican, NM Political Report, and others, as well as official statements from NMOGA, AFP, and the Clear Horizons NM coalition.

Limitations

  • Spend figures are ranges, not exact amounts. Meta reports spending in bands (e.g., $700‚Äì$799). All totals in this report use the sum of individual ad ranges.
  • Keyword searches may miss ads that use different terminology, are image/video-only, or don't include searchable text content. Several Clear Horizons NM ads had no text captured in the API.
  • Demographic and geographic data reflects delivery, not targeting. Skews in who saw an ad can be caused by first-party data, algorithmic optimization, creative resonance, and platform demographics ‚Äî not just the advertiser's targeting selections.
  • Meta-only coverage. This report does not include TV, radio, Google/YouTube, programmatic display, direct mail, or offline advertising activity, all of which are likely significant for this issue.
  • ProgressNow NM's total spend ($4,900‚Äì$7,482) includes many ads unrelated to SB 18 (Albuquerque city council, congressional advocacy). Only $1,500‚Äì$1,999 was directly SB 18-related.
