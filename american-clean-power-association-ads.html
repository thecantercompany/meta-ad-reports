Strategic Takeaway

The American Clean Power Association (ACP), the nation’s largest clean energy trade association, ran 164 political and issue ads on Meta platforms from February 2024 through December 2025, spending an estimated $80K–$115K. ACP’s advertising strategy evolved through six distinct phases—from defending green hydrogen tax rules to mobilizing a grassroots “PowerVotes” movement—revealing how the industry’s premier lobby adapted its messaging as clean energy policy shifted from the IRA implementation era into an existential defense of tax credits under the incoming Trump administration and the One Big Beautiful Bill.

Organization Profile

Who Is the American Clean Power Association?

ACP is the unified national trade association for the clean energy industry, representing over 800 companies across wind, solar, energy storage, clean hydrogen, and transmission.

Founded
2021, through the merger of the American Wind Energy Association (AWEA), the Energy Storage Association (ESA), and the U.S. Solar Energy Industries Association’s utility-scale division.
Tax Status

CEO
Jason Grumet (since January 2023). Former founder and 15-year president of the Bipartisan Policy Center. Known for cross-aisle relationship-building.
Revenue
~$35 million annually. Funded by member dues from developers, manufacturers, utilities, financial institutions, and technology providers.
Key Members
AES Clean Energy, Avangrid Renewables, BHE Renewables, Clearway Energy, Dominion Energy, EDF Renewables, Southern Power, Amazon Web Services, American Electric Power.
Lobbying
$2.01 million spent on federal lobbying in 2024 (OpenSecrets). Focus areas include IRA implementation, permitting reform, and grid infrastructure.

Policy Context: A Pivotal Two Years

ACP’s advertising in this period maps directly onto the clean energy industry’s most consequential policy window since the IRA’s passage in August 2022. The two years covered by this report span:

Early 2024: Treasury rulemakings on 45V clean hydrogen tax credits that would determine whether the green hydrogen industry could scale. Fall 2024: The presidential election, with IRA tax credits on the ballot by proxy. January 2025: Trump’s return to office and executive orders pausing IRA disbursements. Mid-2025: Congressional debate on the “One Big Beautiful Bill,” which ultimately sunsets many IRA clean energy tax credits by December 2025. Late 2025: ACP pivots to grassroots mobilization via PowerVotes as the legislative threat materializes.

Spending Pattern Analysis

Two spending peaks dominate: January 2025 ($35K) coinciding with the Trump inauguration and offshore wind / battery storage defense campaigns, and November–December 2025 ($35K combined) when the PowerVotes grassroots mobilization launched as the One Big Beautiful Bill threatened IRA tax credits. The September 2024 Iowa kids campaign ($18K) represents an emotional storytelling approach unique to ACP’s otherwise policy-focused advertising.

Campaign-by-Campaign Analysis

Six Distinct Advertising Phases

ACP’s 164 ads break into six clearly defined campaigns, each tied to a specific policy moment and employing distinct messaging strategies.

Phase 1 • Policy Advocacy

Green Hydrogen 45V Tax Credit Defense

Feb – Apr 2024

ACP’s first 2024 campaign targeted the Treasury Department’s proposed rules for the Section 45V clean hydrogen production tax credit. Treasury’s “time-matching” requirements—which would require hydrogen producers to match their electricity consumption with clean energy generation on an hourly basis—threatened to make green hydrogen economically unviable. ACP argued the proposal would cut deployment by 53%.

The ads were geographically concentrated in California, Illinois, Minnesota, Washington, and the D.C. metro—a pattern consistent with targeting policymakers and clean energy workforce hubs rather than the general public.

“Treasury decisions on time-matching requirements will determine whether a U.S. green hydrogen industry moves forward in the next decade. Treasury’s current proposal decreases green hydrogen deployment by 53 percent.”

Treasury released final 45V rules in January 2025 with significant flexibility concessions, allowing annual (rather than hourly) emissions accounting—a partial win for ACP’s position. Phase 2 • Awareness / Storytelling

Iowa “Kids Love Clean Energy” Campaign

Sep – Oct 2024

ACP’s single largest ad burst by volume: 34 ads in September alone, nearly all promoting the same two video creatives featuring Iowa children talking about clean energy. Iowa, which generates more electricity from wind than any other state by percentage, served as the campaign’s proof point. The timing—six weeks before the 2024 presidential election—positioned clean energy as a bipartisan, family-friendly issue.

The ads were geo-targeted to Georgia and other swing/Southern states, not Iowa itself. This is a persuasion campaign aimed at audiences in states where clean energy may be perceived as politically contentious.

Phase 3 • Economic Messaging

Energy Tax Credits & State Economic Impact

Oct 2024

Immediately following the Iowa campaign, ACP launched a state-by-state economic impact blitz with 43 ads across 15+ states. Each ad was customized with state-specific statistics: jobs created, billions added to local economies, and power capacity delivered. This was the most geographically distributed campaign ACP ran—reaching states from Texas to North Carolina to Illinois to Iowa.

The messaging framed energy tax credits as economic drivers, not environmental policy. This is strategic: by grounding the argument in jobs and investment, ACP made the case for preserving IRA credits in language that appeals across partisan lines.

Pre-Election Timing

Phases 2 and 3 together represent ACP’s most intensive advertising period: 77 ads between September and October 2024, timed to saturate audiences before the November election. This was not candidate advertising, but it was clearly designed to build public support for clean energy policies that both candidates’ platforms would impact. The industry was hedging—building a constituency that would pressure whichever administration took office to preserve tax credits.

Phase 4 • Sector Defense

Offshore Wind & Battery Storage Campaigns

Dec 2024 – Feb 2025

ACP’s highest single-month spend ($35K in January 2025) came just days after Trump took office. The campaign split into two tracks:

Offshore wind on the Gulf Coast: Two ads alone spent $10K–$15K each, exclusively geo-targeted to Washington, D.C., promoting the economic benefits of offshore wind for Louisiana and the Gulf. This is textbook grasstops lobbying—placing ads in the feeds of policymakers, Hill staffers, and federal agency employees while the new administration was forming its energy agenda. The messaging leaned into economic framing: “new opportunities for a longtime energy hub.”

Battery storage in California: Concurrent ads highlighted BESS jobs (53,000+ Californians), grid reliability during heat waves, and $100 billion in investment commitments. These ran statewide in California, supporting ACP-California’s legislative agenda.

D.C. Geofencing as Lobbying

The offshore wind ads’ 100% delivery to Washington, D.C. is the clearest signal of ACP’s advertising intent. At $10K–$15K per ad targeting only the D.C. metro, these are not mass awareness buys—they are digital lobbying, placing pro-industry messaging into the social media feeds of executive branch officials, congressional staff, and agency decision-makers during the first weeks of the new administration. This pattern is common among trade associations but rarely this precisely documented.

Phase 5 • Economic Narrative

Manufacturing Boom & Surging Energy Demand

Apr – Aug 2025

As the One Big Beautiful Bill moved through Congress and threatened IRA tax credits, ACP shifted to economic nationalism messaging. Two parallel narratives ran simultaneously:

“Manufacturing boom” ads emphasized that clean energy was driving U.S. industrial growth, creating “good-paying jobs,” and building “a stronger, more reliable energy future.” The framing deliberately mirrors the economic messaging used to pass the IRA originally.

“Energy demand is surging” ads were state-specific (Oklahoma, Iowa, Louisiana, West Virginia, North Carolina) and argued that rising energy demand requires “all energy sources”—an “all-of-the-above” framing designed to appeal to fossil fuel state legislators who might otherwise oppose clean energy provisions.

“All-of-the-Above” Framing

The choice to run “surging demand” ads in Oklahoma, West Virginia, Louisiana, Iowa, and North Carolina—all states with significant fossil fuel industries and Republican representation—reflects a deliberate cross-aisle strategy. By framing clean energy as complementary to existing energy production rather than a replacement for it, ACP was lobbying Republican senators and House members from energy states who would vote on the reconciliation bill. The message wasn’t “replace fossil fuels”—it was “we need everything.”

Deep Dive — Phase 6

The PowerVotes Campaign

ACP’s most expensive campaign represents a strategic inflection point: the moment an industry trade association pivoted from lobbying policymakers to mobilizing a grassroots constituency. Nine ads, $27K–$35K in spend, one message—and a revealing set of contradictions.

What Is PowerVotes?

PowerVotes — powervotes.com

PowerVotes is ACP’s grassroots advocacy platform, built to convert passive clean energy supporters into active political participants. The platform asks visitors to:

  • Join the advocacy network by providing contact information
  • Contact members of Congress to support permitting reform and pro-energy policies
  • Urge lawmakers to “support policies that secure a strong American energy future backed by a broad range of energy projects”

The site emphasizes protecting “more than a million American clean energy jobs” and claims the clean energy workforce will double between 2025 and 2030. It targets five stakeholder groups: employees, supply chain partners, veterans, landowners, and community allies.

Key policy ask: Permitting reform that accelerates energy project approvals and keeps costs low. The platform deliberately frames its ask in “all-of-the-above” energy language rather than clean-energy-specific terms. Sources: powervotes.com, ACP Grassroots Strategy Session

The OBBB Had Already Passed

A critical detail: the One Big Beautiful Bill was signed into law on July 4, 2025—nearly five months before ACP launched PowerVotes ads on Meta. The bill’s clean energy provisions were already enacted: wind and solar projects became ineligible for IRA tax credits unless they begin construction within 12 months of passage or enter service before December 31, 2027. Clean hydrogen credits terminate for projects starting after December 31, 2027.

This means the PowerVotes Meta campaign was not trying to stop legislation—it was trying to build a constituency for the fights ahead: regulatory implementation, potential future legislation to restore credits, and 2026 midterm elections. The campaign was forward-looking, not defensive. Sources: Davis Wright Tremaine, Tax Foundation

Staging URL Reveals Rushed Launch

Two of the earliest PowerVotes ads (launched Nov 24) pointed users to powervotes.webflow.io—a Webflow staging URL, not the production domain. The highest-spend ad (ID: 4269478523315243, $10K–$15K) reached an audience that was 82% aged 65 and older. Males 65+ alone comprised 37.2% of delivery; females 65+ comprised 44.4%. Adults under 45 made up barely 5% of impressions.

Algorithm vs. Intent: The Demographic Question

There are three possible explanations for the extreme 65+ skew, and they carry different strategic implications:

1. Algorithmic optimization. Meta’s delivery algorithm may have determined that 65+ users were most likely to click “Join the PowerVotes Movement,” optimizing delivery toward them. Political action CTAs historically perform well with older, more politically engaged users on Facebook. If this is the driver, ACP may not have intended or desired this audience concentration.

2. Broad targeting with no age restrictions. ACP may have targeted broadly (national, all ages) and let Meta optimize. The result—heavy 65+ delivery—reflects Facebook’s aging user base and the tendency for older users to engage with political issue content. This is a known platform dynamic that advocacy campaigns on Meta must account for.

3. Intentional retiree targeting. ACP may have calculated that retirees are more likely to contact Congress (they have time), more likely to vote in midterms, and more likely to be landowners in wind/solar development areas. If intentional, this is a strategic choice to build a politically active constituency from voters with high civic participation rates.

Regardless of cause, the outcome is the same: a “grassroots movement” for clean energy workers is overwhelmingly reaching retirees. ACP’s PowerVotes platform claims to mobilize “employees, supply chain partners, veterans, [and] landowners,” but the Meta data suggests the actual audience skews far older than the working-age population the messaging describes. This mismatch between stated audience and actual delivery is the campaign’s most significant strategic gap.

Texas Dominance Is Not Random

Texas consistently captured 10–14% of PowerVotes impressions—roughly 3x its share of the U.S. Facebook user base. Texas is simultaneously the largest wind energy producer in the country, a state whose Republican congressional delegation has significant influence over energy policy, and a state where clean energy is woven into the rural economy through landowner lease payments. ACP’s algorithm-aided concentration in Texas aligns with a strategic need to demonstrate grassroots clean energy support in a state that “reads” as politically red. A PowerVotes sign-up from Texas carries more persuasive weight with a Republican lawmaker than one from California.

The Virginia/Maryland Signal

Virginia and Maryland combined for ~5.7% of PowerVotes delivery—significantly above their population share. This is the D.C. metro spillover effect: even in a “national” campaign, ACP’s policymaker-adjacent geography pulls disproportionate delivery. Some of these impressions may be reaching the same Hill staffers and agency employees that ACP’s January 2025 offshore wind ads geofenced directly. The trade association’s inside game and outside game overlap geographically.

Cost Efficiency Divergence

The November and December high-spend ads showed dramatically different cost efficiency:

Ad #5 (Nov 24–Dec 5): $3.5K–$4K spend for 300K–350K impressions = ~$12 CPM. This was the most efficient PowerVotes ad by a wide margin.

Ad #9 (Dec 19–25): $10K–$15K spend for 100K–125K impressions = ~$110 CPM. This was nearly 10x more expensive per impression.

The December ads paid a steep premium for delivery. Holiday-season CPMs on Meta typically spike, but a 10x increase suggests either aggressive frequency capping, a narrow audience that was quickly saturated, or Meta’s algorithm struggling to find cost-effective delivery for a political issue ad during peak commercial advertising season. ACP may have been paying Christmas-week rates to push a grassroots message that would have been dramatically cheaper in November.

PowerVotes Campaign Assessment

Campaign Summary

PowerVotes on Meta: Strategic Escalation with Structural Limitations

The PowerVotes Meta campaign represents a meaningful strategic shift for ACP—from talking to policymakers to trying to activate a constituency. But the execution reveals several tensions:

Strength — Speed and commitment. ACP went from zero to $35K in spend within four weeks, demonstrating organizational willingness to invest in a new campaign framework. The PowerVotes platform itself is substantive, with specific policy asks (permitting reform) and clear calls to action (contact Congress).

Weakness — Audience mismatch. A “movement” campaign for clean energy workers is overwhelmingly reaching retirees. Whether by algorithm or design, the Meta execution is not building the diverse coalition (employees, veterans, supply chain workers) that PowerVotes describes.

Weakness — Creative uniformity. Nine ads with identical copy and no visual variation limits Meta’s ability to optimize across audience segments. An A/B test framework with varied messaging—economic framing for some audiences, patriotic framing for others—would likely improve both reach distribution and cost efficiency.

Weakness — Timing premium. Launching a scaled push during Christmas week cost ACP dearly in CPMs. The December ads were roughly 10x less efficient than the November ads. Starting the campaign 4–6 weeks earlier would have delivered more impressions for the same budget.

Question — Post-OBBB strategy. The OBBB was already law when PowerVotes ads ran. ACP appears to be building infrastructure for longer-term advocacy rather than trying to influence an immediate vote. This is strategically sound but requires sustained investment to mature. The abrupt stop after December 25 raises the question: was this the start of an ongoing campaign, or a one-time surge that has already ended?

A Textbook Inside-Outside Strategy

ACP’s geographic evolution over two years maps a classic advocacy playbook: start with targeted regulatory influence (D.C., industry hubs), layer in public awareness (swing states before an election), shift to economic persuasion (energy-producing states with wavering legislators), and culminate in broad grassroots mobilization when legislative action becomes imminent. Each phase built on the last, expanding the audience while refining the message for each constituency.

Three narrative strategies dominate ACP’s messaging:

1. Economic Nationalism

The most persistent theme across all campaigns. Words like “manufacturing,” “jobs,” “boom,” “American-made,” and “domestic” appear consistently. ACP frames clean energy not as environmental policy but as industrial policy—a job creator, a manufacturing driver, and a pathway to energy security. This framing is intentionally bipartisan and mirrors the language used to pass the IRA with Republican support from manufacturing districts.

2. Grid Reliability & Energy Security

“Reliable,” “demand,” “surging,” and “stronger” signal ACP’s effort to counter the narrative that renewables are intermittent and unreliable. The “all energy sources” framing in the demand-surging ads positions clean energy as complementary to the existing grid, not disruptive to it. Battery storage is explicitly positioned as the reliability solution.

3. Collective Action

The PowerVotes campaign introduces urgency and community language: “work together,” “join,” “movement.” This represents a tonal shift from ACP’s earlier informational and economic messaging toward emotional mobilization—a signal that the organization perceives the political threat as severe enough to warrant activating its base.

What ACP Doesn’t Say

Notable absences in ACP’s messaging: the words “climate,” “emissions,” “carbon,” and “environment” appear in virtually zero ads. This is a deliberate choice. ACP has completely decoupled its public-facing advertising from climate science framing, instead positioning clean energy purely in economic, security, and patriotic terms. The trade association representing the industry most directly affected by climate policy has calculated that climate language is a political liability in its Meta advertising.

Compliance Notes

Ad Transparency Observations

Several ACP ads were flagged by Meta with the notice: “This ad ran without a required disclaimer.” At least 8 ads across August 2024, September 2024, April 2025, and July 2025 carried this flag. Meta requires political and issue ads to include “paid for by” disclaimers. While the flagged ads appear to have low spend ($0–$199), the recurring pattern suggests either a workflow issue in ACP’s ad operations or ads that were initially posted as non-political content and subsequently classified as issue advertising by Meta’s enforcement system.

One ad from January 2025 was removed entirely: “This content was removed because it didn’t follow our Advertising Standards.” (View record)

Strategic Assessment

What This Spending Pattern Reveals

ACP’s $80K–$115K in Meta advertising is modest by Washington trade association standards—particularly for an organization with $35 million in annual revenue and $2 million in lobbying spend. Meta is clearly not ACP’s primary advertising channel. But the platform’s value to ACP lies in its targeting precision: the ability to place specific messages in front of specific audiences at specific policy moments.

Several strategic patterns emerge:

Meta as a surgical tool, not a mass medium. ACP uses Facebook and Instagram for targeted influence operations—D.C. geofencing for policymakers, state-specific ads for legislators, swing-state persuasion before elections. This is a sophisticated, if small-budget, digital advocacy operation.

The absence of climate framing is a strategic calculation. For the industry’s largest trade association to avoid climate language entirely in its paid advertising reflects a judgment about political reality: that clean energy’s best argument is economic, not environmental. This may limit ACP’s appeal to its environmental base but expands its reach to the center-right lawmakers whose votes matter most for tax credit survival.

The PowerVotes pivot signals escalation. The shift from inside-game targeting (D.C., state capitals) to outside-game grassroots mobilization (national PowerVotes campaign) in late 2025 suggests ACP concluded that lobbying alone would not protect IRA tax credits. The willingness to spend $35K in two months on movement-building—more than any prior period—indicates the organization views the policy threat as existential for its members.

ACP went dark after December 2025. No ads have run since late December. This could reflect a strategic pause after the One Big Beautiful Bill’s passage, budget reallocation to other channels, or a reassessment of Meta’s utility as a platform. The silence itself is data.

Methodology & Data Limitations

This report analyzes political and issue advertising by the American Clean Power Association (Page ID: 9423488774) on Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram) from February 6, 2024 through February 6, 2026.

  • Data source: Meta Ad Library API, accessed via the Meta Ad Library MCP server on February 6, 2026.

  • Impression figures: Similarly reported in ranges by Meta.
  • Demographics: Meta’s demographic data reflects who saw the ads (delivery), not who was targeted. Demographic skews may reflect algorithmic optimization, not advertiser intent.
  • Geographic delivery: Based on Meta’s delivery_by_region data. Percentages reflect relative reach within each ad, not absolute audience size.
  • Keyword search: Ads were retrieved via page-level queries, not keyword searches. The 164 ad count represents all political/issue ads from this page in the specified period.
  • Scope: This report covers only Meta platform advertising.
