Strategic Takeaway

The American Clean Power Association (ACP), the nation’s largest clean energy trade association, ran 164 political and issue ads on Meta platforms from February 2024 through December 2025, spending an estimated $80K–$115K. ACP’s advertising strategy evolved through six distinct phases—from defending green hydrogen tax rules to mobilizing a grassroots “PowerVotes” movement—revealing how the industry’s premier lobby adapted its messaging as clean energy policy shifted from the IRA implementation era into a two-front war: defending tax credits against the One Big Beautiful Bill, then pivoting to permitting reform via the bipartisan SPEED Act. The PowerVotes campaign, ACP’s most expensive initiative, launched the same week ACP endorsed the SPEED Act—and kept running even after ACP withdrew that endorsement amid bipartisan backlash.

Campaign-by-Campaign Analysis

Six Distinct Advertising Phases

ACP’s 164 ads break into six clearly defined campaigns, each tied to a specific policy moment and employing distinct messaging strategies.

Phase 1 • Policy Advocacy

Green Hydrogen 45V Tax Credit Defense

Feb – Apr 2024

ACP’s first 2024 campaign targeted the Treasury Department’s proposed rules for the Section 45V clean hydrogen production tax credit. Treasury’s “time-matching” requirements—which would require hydrogen producers to match their electricity consumption with clean energy generation on an hourly basis—threatened to make green hydrogen economically unviable. ACP argued the proposal would cut deployment by 53%.

The ads were geographically concentrated in California, Illinois, Minnesota, Washington, and the D.C. metro—a pattern consistent with targeting policymakers and clean energy workforce hubs rather than the general public.

“Treasury decisions on time-matching requirements will determine whether a U.S. green hydrogen industry moves forward in the next decade. Treasury’s current proposal decreases green hydrogen deployment by 53 percent.”

Pre-Election Timing

Phases 2 and 3 together represent ACP’s most intensive advertising period: 77 ads between September and October 2024, timed to saturate audiences before the November election. Phase 4 • Sector Defense

Offshore Wind & Battery Storage Campaigns

Dec 2024 – Feb 2025

ACP’s highest single-month spend ($35K in January 2025) came just days after Trump took office. The campaign split into two tracks:

Offshore wind on the Gulf Coast: Two ads alone spent $10K–$15K each, exclusively geo-targeted to Washington, D.C., promoting the economic benefits of offshore wind for Louisiana and the Gulf. The messaging leaned into economic framing: “new opportunities for a longtime energy hub.”

Battery storage in California: Concurrent ads highlighted BESS jobs (53,000+ Californians), grid reliability during heat waves, and $100 billion in investment commitments. These ran statewide in California, supporting ACP-California’s legislative agenda.

D.C. Geofencing as Lobbying

The offshore wind ads’ 100% delivery to Washington, D.C. is the clearest signal of ACP’s advertising intent. At $10K–$15K per ad targeting only the D.C. metro, these are not mass awareness buys—they are digital lobbying, placing pro-industry messaging into the social media feeds of executive branch officials, congressional staff, and agency decision-makers during the first weeks of the new administration. This pattern is common among trade associations but rarely this precisely documented.

Phase 5 • Economic Narrative

Manufacturing Boom & Surging Energy Demand

Apr – Aug 2025

As the One Big Beautiful Bill moved through Congress and threatened IRA tax credits, ACP shifted to economic nationalism messaging. Two parallel narratives ran simultaneously:

“Manufacturing boom” ads emphasized that clean energy was driving U.S. industrial growth, creating “good-paying jobs,” and building “a stronger, more reliable energy future.” The framing deliberately mirrors the economic messaging used to pass the IRA originally.

“All-of-the-Above” Framing

The choice to run “surging demand” ads in Oklahoma, West Virginia, Louisiana, Iowa, and North Carolina—all states with significant fossil fuel industries and Republican representation—reflects a deliberate cross-aisle strategy. By framing clean energy as complementary to existing energy production rather than a replacement for it, ACP was lobbying Republican senators and House members from energy states who would vote on the reconciliation bill. The message wasn’t “replace fossil fuels”—it was “we need everything.”

PowerVotes: Largest Single Campaign by Spend Velocity

While the Offshore Wind + Battery Storage phase had comparable total spend (~31% vs. ~30%), it ran across three months with 24 ads. PowerVotes achieved nearly the same outlay in just four weeks with only 9 ads—making it the highest-velocity spend in ACP’s entire two-year ad history. The average PowerVotes ad spent $3,000–$3,900 vs. $490–$670 for all other campaigns. ACP was writing bigger checks, faster, for this campaign than anything before it.

What Is PowerVotes?

PowerVotes — powervotes.com

PowerVotes is ACP’s grassroots advocacy platform, built to convert passive clean energy supporters into active political participants. The platform asks visitors to:

  • Join the advocacy network by providing contact information
  • Contact members of Congress to support permitting reform and pro-energy policies
  • Urge lawmakers to “support policies that secure a strong American energy future backed by a broad range of energy projects”

Despite being run by a trade association, the site deliberately frames itself as a broad populist movement rather than an industry campaign. The homepage calls PowerVotes “the unified voice of America’s workers, families, businesses, and communities.” Jobs are mentioned (“more than a million American clean energy jobs”), but they’re one argument among many—alongside energy costs, reliability, and national security. The target audience is anyone who cares about affordable energy, not just people who work in the sector.

Key policy ask: Permitting reform that accelerates energy project approvals and keeps costs low. The platform deliberately frames its ask in “all-of-the-above” energy language rather than clean-energy-specific terms. Sources: powervotes.com, ACP Grassroots Strategy Session

Legislative Context: Why November 2025?

The previous version of this report incorrectly framed the PowerVotes campaign as connected to the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBB). It wasn’t. The OBBB was signed into law on July 4, 2025—nearly five months before PowerVotes launched. And the ads themselves never mention OBBB, tax credits, or reconciliation.

The real trigger was the SPEED Act—a bipartisan permitting reform bill that ACP had been lobbying for and that moved through committee in November 2025. The PowerVotes ads explicitly call for “permitting reform that will help meet demand and keep costs for families and businesses low.” The timing is precise:

The SPEED Act Controversy

The Simplify Permitting and Enable Efficient Development (SPEED) Act aimed to overhaul NEPA review timelines for energy projects. ACP initially backed it as the permitting reform the industry needed. But on December 17, the House Rules Committee adopted an amendment making clear that certain offshore wind projects remained vulnerable to permit revocation by the Trump administration. ACP CEO Jason Grumet reversed the organization’s endorsement, saying the amendment “fundamentally eviscerated” the bill’s technology-neutral framework.

The backlash was bipartisan and sharp. Rep. Jared Golden (D-Maine), a co-sponsor, called ACP’s withdrawal “awfully shortsighted.” Sen. Brian Schatz (D-Hawaii) was angry ACP had been cutting deals with the American Petroleum Institute behind the scenes. Other clean energy groups—the Clean Energy Buyers Association, ACORE, and the National Ocean Industries Association—expressed disappointment but maintained their support, leaving ACP isolated in its reversal.

The bill passed the House with bipartisan support on December 19. ACP’s PowerVotes ads continued running through December 25, still calling for “permitting reform.” Notably, the ads never named the SPEED Act—the messaging operated at the issue level, not the bill level. Sources: E&E News/POLITICO, ACP Statement, Solar Power World

Why the Ads Kept Running

A critical detail: the PowerVotes ads never mentioned the SPEED Act by name. Neither does the PowerVotes website. The call to action was always generic—“permitting reform that will help meet demand and keep costs for families and businesses low.” ACP’s withdrawal from one specific bill didn’t make the ads inaccurate or contradictory. “Permitting reform” as an issue didn’t die with ACP’s SPEED Act reversal; it remained (and remains) ACP’s top legislative priority.

This actually suggests deliberate message design. By keeping the ads issue-level rather than bill-level, ACP built a grassroots list that stays relevant regardless of which specific permitting bill is moving through Congress. The supporters who signed up for PowerVotes in November are still contactable for the next permitting fight—whether that’s a revised SPEED Act, a standalone bill, or provisions attached to other legislation. The SPEED Act was the catalyst for the ad campaign’s timing, but ACP was smart enough not to tie the messaging to it.

Algorithm vs. Intent: The Demographic Question

There are three possible explanations for the extreme 65+ skew, and they carry different strategic implications:

1. Algorithmic optimization. Meta’s delivery algorithm may have determined that 65+ users were most likely to click “Join the PowerVotes Movement,” optimizing delivery toward them. Political action CTAs historically perform well with older, more politically engaged users on Facebook. If this is the driver, ACP may not have intended or desired this audience concentration.

2. Broad targeting with no age restrictions. ACP may have targeted broadly (national, all ages) and let Meta optimize. The result—heavy 65+ delivery—reflects Facebook’s aging user base and the tendency for older users to engage with political issue content. This is a known platform dynamic that advocacy campaigns on Meta must account for.

3. Intentional retiree targeting. ACP may have calculated that retirees are more likely to contact Congress (they have time), more likely to vote in midterms, and more likely to be landowners in wind/solar development areas. If intentional, this is a strategic choice to build a politically active constituency from voters with high civic participation rates.

Regardless of cause, the outcome is the same: a populist “grassroots movement” claiming to represent workers, families, and communities is overwhelmingly reaching retirees. The PowerVotes website positions itself as the voice of a broad coalition, but the Meta delivery data shows a concentrated 65+ audience. This isn’t necessarily a failure—retirees are high-propensity voters and effective congressional contacts—but it does mean the “movement” is narrower in practice than the branding suggests. The question is whether ACP is aware of this gap and considers it acceptable, or whether the platform dynamics are undermining their stated goal of a cross-generational coalition.

Texas Dominance Is Not Random

Texas consistently captured 10–14% of PowerVotes impressions—roughly 3x its share of the U.S. Facebook user base. Texas is simultaneously the largest wind energy producer in the country, a state whose Republican congressional delegation has significant influence over energy policy, and a state where clean energy is woven into the rural economy through landowner lease payments. ACP’s algorithm-aided concentration in Texas aligns with a strategic need to demonstrate grassroots clean energy support in a state that “reads” as politically red. A PowerVotes sign-up from Texas carries more persuasive weight with a Republican lawmaker than one from California.

The Virginia/Maryland Signal

Virginia and Maryland combined for ~5.7% of PowerVotes delivery—significantly above their population share. This is the D.C. metro spillover effect: even in a “national” campaign, ACP’s policymaker-adjacent geography pulls disproportionate delivery. Some of these impressions may be reaching the same Hill staffers and agency employees that ACP’s January 2025 offshore wind ads geofenced directly. The trade association’s inside game and outside game overlap geographically.

Cost Efficiency Divergence

The November and December high-spend ads showed dramatically different cost efficiency:

Ad #5 (Nov 24–Dec 5): $3.5K–$4K spend for 300K–350K impressions = ~$12 CPM. This was the most efficient PowerVotes ad by a wide margin.

Ad #9 (Dec 19–25): $10K–$15K spend for 100K–125K impressions = ~$110 CPM. This was nearly 10x more expensive per impression.

The December ads paid a steep premium for delivery. Holiday-season CPMs on Meta typically spike, but a 10x increase suggests either aggressive frequency capping, a narrow audience that was quickly saturated, or Meta’s algorithm struggling to find cost-effective delivery for a political issue ad during peak commercial advertising season. ACP may have been paying Christmas-week rates to push a grassroots message that would have been dramatically cheaper in November.

PowerVotes Campaign Assessment

Campaign Summary

PowerVotes on Meta: Strategic Escalation with Structural Limitations

The PowerVotes Meta campaign represents a meaningful strategic shift for ACP—from talking to policymakers to trying to activate a constituency. But the execution reveals several tensions:

Strength — Speed and commitment. ACP went from zero to $35K in spend within four weeks, demonstrating organizational willingness to invest in a new campaign framework. The PowerVotes platform itself is substantive, with specific policy asks (permitting reform) and clear calls to action (contact Congress).

Weakness — Audience concentration. A broad grassroots campaign pitched to “workers, families, businesses, and communities” is overwhelmingly reaching adults 65+. Whether by algorithm or design, the Meta execution is concentrating on retirees rather than building the diverse, cross-generational coalition that PowerVotes describes. The 65+ skew may actually align with an effective grassroots strategy (retirees vote, contact Congress, and own land in project areas), but it contradicts the populist “everyone” framing of the website and ads.

Weakness — Creative uniformity. Nine ads with identical copy and no visual variation limits Meta’s ability to optimize across audience segments. An A/B test framework with varied messaging—economic framing for some audiences, patriotic framing for others—would likely improve both reach distribution and cost efficiency.

Weakness — Timing premium. Launching a scaled push during Christmas week cost ACP dearly in CPMs. The December ads were roughly 10x less efficient than the November ads. Starting the campaign 4–6 weeks earlier would have delivered more impressions for the same budget.

Strength — Issue-level messaging. The PowerVotes ads never named the SPEED Act or any specific legislation. By keeping the call to action at the issue level (“permitting reform”), ACP built a grassroots list that survives any single bill’s fate. When ACP withdrew its SPEED Act endorsement on December 17, the ads didn’t become contradictory—they were still accurate. This is savvy campaign design: the SPEED Act fight provided the urgency to launch, but the messaging was durable enough to outlast it. The grassroots contacts collected in November and December remain deployable for whatever permitting vehicle emerges next.

A Textbook Inside-Outside Strategy

ACP’s geographic evolution over two years maps a classic advocacy playbook: start with targeted regulatory influence (D.C., industry hubs), layer in public awareness (swing states before an election), shift to economic persuasion (energy-producing states with wavering legislators), and culminate in broad grassroots mobilization when legislative action becomes imminent. Each phase built on the last, expanding the audience while refining the message for each constituency.

This framing choice may limit appeal to the environmental base but expands reach to the center-right lawmakers whose votes matter most for tax credit survival.

Compliance Notes

Ad Transparency Observations

Several ACP ads were flagged by Meta with the notice: “This ad ran without a required disclaimer.” At least 8 ads across August 2024, September 2024, April 2025, and July 2025 carried this flag. Meta requires political and issue ads to include “paid for by” disclaimers. While the flagged ads appear to have low spend ($0–$199), the recurring pattern suggests either a workflow issue in ACP’s ad operations or ads that were initially posted as non-political content and subsequently classified as issue advertising by Meta’s enforcement system.

One ad from January 2025 was removed entirely: “This content was removed because it didn’t follow our Advertising Standards.” (View record)

Strategic Assessment

What This Spending Pattern Reveals

ACP’s $80K–$115K in Meta advertising is modest by Washington trade association standards—particularly for an organization with $35 million in annual revenue and $2 million in lobbying spend. Meta is clearly not ACP’s primary advertising channel. But the platform’s value to ACP lies in its targeting precision: the ability to place specific messages in front of specific audiences at specific policy moments.

🎯

Meta as a Surgical Tool

ACP uses Facebook/Instagram for targeted influence operations—D.C. geofencing for policymakers, state-specific ads for legislators, swing-state persuasion before elections. Sophisticated, small-budget digital advocacy.

🚫

Climate Silence as Strategy

Zero mentions of “climate,” “emissions,” or “carbon” across 164 ads. ACP has calculated that clean energy’s best argument is economic, not environmental. Limits appeal to the green base; expands reach to center-right lawmakers.

📢

PowerVotes: Smart Message Architecture

The shift from inside-game (D.C. geofencing) to outside-game (national grassroots) was triggered by the SPEED Act fight but designed to outlast it. By keeping ads at the issue level (“permitting reform”) rather than naming specific legislation, ACP built a reusable grassroots list. The $35K investment created infrastructure for the next permitting battle, not just this one.

💤

ACP Went Dark After Dec 2025

No ads since late December. The SPEED Act controversy, bipartisan backlash against ACP’s tactics, and the holiday season all may have contributed to the pause. The silence itself is data—does ACP view Meta as spent, or is it regrouping for a revised permitting push and the 2026 midterms?

Methodology & Data Limitations

This report analyzes political and issue advertising by the American Clean Power Association (Page ID: 9423488774) on Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram) from February 6, 2024 through February 6, 2026.

  • Data source: Meta Ad Library API, accessed via the Meta Ad Library MCP server on February 6, 2026.
  • Spend figures: Meta reports spend in ranges (e.g., $100–$199), not exact amounts. Upper-bound estimates are used in timeline charts; ranges are shown in campaign analyses.
  • Impression figures: Similarly reported in ranges by Meta.
  • Demographics: Meta’s demographic data reflects who saw the ads (delivery), not who was targeted. Demographic skews may reflect algorithmic optimization, not advertiser intent.
  • Geographic delivery: Based on Meta’s delivery_by_region data. Percentages reflect relative reach within each ad, not absolute audience size.
  • Keyword search: Ads were retrieved via page-level queries, not keyword searches. The 164 ad count represents all political/issue ads from this page in the specified period.
  • Scope: This report covers only Meta platform advertising. ACP’s broader advocacy spending—including lobbying ($2M+ annually), events, digital advertising on other platforms, and earned media—is not reflected here. Meta advertising represents a small fraction of ACP’s total communications budget.
  • Background research: Contextual information on ACP’s organizational structure, policy positions, and the legislative environment was gathered from public sources including cleanpower.org, OpenSecrets.org, Treasury Department press releases, and news reporting.
