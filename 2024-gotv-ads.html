The Big Picture

Outside groups outspent official campaigns on Meta GOTV advertising by nearly 2-to-1 during the 2024 general election. The Trump campaign dropped $3.4M–$4.6M in just five days, but organizations like Forward Blue, Voto Latino, Sendthevote, and Powered by People ran sustained multi-week operations that collectively dwarfed direct campaign spending. On the progressive side, the outside-group ecosystem was deep and diversified. On the conservative side, it was thinner — concentrated in Turning Point Action and a handful of PACs — revealing a structural asymmetry in how each coalition mobilizes voters on Meta.

01

The GOTV Landscape

The 2024 general election GOTV operation on Meta was shaped by an extraordinary event: President Biden's withdrawal on July 21 and Vice President Harris's rapid consolidation of the Democratic nomination. This created a compressed timeline that benefited outside groups with existing infrastructure while forcing the new Harris campaign to build a Meta ad operation from scratch in under 100 days.

Our analysis tracked 15+ major advertisers running GOTV-oriented ads on Meta between August 1 and November 5, 2024. We categorized them into two buckets per the user's framework:

Campaigns

Definition: Official candidate committees for Biden/Harris and Trump/Vance at the presidential level, plus all down-ballot candidate committees (Colin Allred, etc.)

Key characteristic: Tend to mix GOTV with fundraising and persuasion. Concentrated spending bursts in final weeks.

Outside Groups

Definition: Everything else — super PACs, party committees (DNC/RNC), 501(c)(4)s, civic organizations, advocacy nonprofits, and individual advocates.

Key characteristic: Longer runways, more specialized targeting. Progressive side had deeper bench of Meta advertisers.

A note on "GOTV" classification Defining what counts as a GOTV ad is inherently subjective. We included any ad with a clear voter mobilization hook: "register to vote," "make a plan," "find your polling place," "Election Day is Nov 5," "vote early," or explicit persuasion-plus-turnout messaging ("vote for X to protect Y"). Pure issue ads without a voting call-to-action were excluded. Many campaign ads blend fundraising with GOTV language — we included these when the GOTV hook was prominent.

Progressive outside groups outspent conservative outside groups on Meta GOTV by approximately 2-to-1. More importantly, the progressive side fielded six identifiable Meta GOTV operations versus three on the conservative side. This depth matters: it allows different organizations to target different demographics, geographies, and messages without competing for the same audience.

The RNC outsourcing gamble Unlike prior cycles where the RNC ran a centralized ground game, the 2024 Republican Party outsourced significant GOTV operations to allied groups like Turning Point Action and America PAC. On Meta, this fragmentation is visible: the Trump campaign itself ran a massive final-week blitz, but the broader conservative GOTV infrastructure was thinner and less coordinated. Post-election reporting questioned whether this decentralized approach cost Republicans at the margins.

Spending Over Time

Weekly GOTV Ad Spend by Key Advertiser

The timeline reveals starkly different strategies:

The Trump Blitz: The Trump campaign was virtually absent from Meta GOTV until the final week of October, when it unleashed $3.4M–$4.6M in five days (Oct 24–28). This was a concentrated, high-intensity push — 5,000 ads in under a week. The messaging was laser-focused on battleground states: "STOP WHAT YOU'RE DOING. Do you want North Carolina going to Kamala?" and "Find your Election Day Voting Location & win this for TRUMP."

The Outside Group Marathon: Groups like Forward Blue and Beto/Powered by People ramped spending steadily from July through October, peaking in the final three weeks but maintaining presence throughout. Voto Latino surged around voter registration deadlines in early October.

The Harris Sprint: The Kamala Harris page had virtually no GOTV presence before October, reflecting the compressed timeline after Biden's July 21 withdrawal. When it came, it was focused: $1.1M–$1.4M concentrated in a single month.

Voto Latino's registration deadline spike Voto Latino spent an estimated $699K–$1M in the single week of September 30 — timed to the Texas voter registration deadline (October 7) and New Mexico's deadline (October 8). This was the most strategically precise spending spike in our dataset: a massive investment timed to the exact moment it could convert into registered voters. After deadlines passed, spending dropped sharply as the organization shifted to turnout messaging.

Texas Spotlight

Texas was the epicenter of down-ballot GOTV activity on Meta, driven primarily by the Colin Allred vs. Ted Cruz Senate race — one of the most expensive Senate contests in the country at $250M+ in total spending across all media.

Texas GOTV Ecosystem

Three distinct GOTV operations converged in Texas:

1. The Allred Machine ($2.7M–$3.3M): Colin Allred's campaign was the largest single Meta advertiser in the Texas GOTV space. While much of his ad spend was fundraising-oriented (targeting national donors), his Texas-delivered ads anchored the Democratic GOTV infrastructure. The campaign opened 30+ field offices across Texas and Allred's digital operation served as the connective tissue for the broader progressive GOTV coalition.

2. Powered by People ($1.1M–$1.4M): Beto O'Rourke's organization was the dedicated Texas GOTV engine. With 0.3% delivery concentration in Texas (the highest of any state), Powered by People ran video-heavy ads focused on voter registration and early voting. The organization reported knocking on 3 million+ doors — Meta ads were one channel in a multi-platform mobilization effort.

3. The Ground-Level Groups ($89K–$125K): Texas Organizing Project focused on down-ballot progressive candidates in Houston, Dallas, and San Antonio. Their ads were hyper-local: specific county judge endorsements, HISD bond opposition, marijuana decriminalization propositions. This is where Meta GOTV connected to the ballot below the fold.

National groups largely skipped Texas Despite the competitive Senate race, national GOTV groups like Sendthevote, HeadCount, and Forward Blue delivered minimal ads to Texas. The Trump campaign didn't target Texas either. The implicit calculation: Texas was not a presidential battleground, and national GOTV dollars were better spent in true swing states. Texas GOTV was left to Texas-specific organizations and the Allred campaign.

The Rio Grande Valley Dimension

Voto Latino was the only major national organization with significant Texas GOTV delivery. Their massive $699K–$1M registration-deadline spending spike in late September/early October included Texas alongside swing states, reflecting the strategic importance of the Latino vote in both the Senate race and competitive House races in the Rio Grande Valley (TX-15, TX-28, TX-34).

The RGV's rightward shift — which began in 2020 and continued in 2024 — made Latino voter engagement a two-way competition. Republican-aligned groups did not appear to mount a significant Meta GOTV operation specifically targeting the RGV, suggesting their voter contact strategy in South Texas relied more heavily on door-to-door canvassing and other channels.

07

New Mexico Spotlight

New Mexico tells a very different story from Texas: despite hosting one of the most competitive House races in the country (NM-2, Vasquez vs. Herrell), Meta GOTV spending in the state was vanishingly small.

NM-2: The Race That Meta GOTV Forgot

The NM-2 rematch between Rep. Gabe Vasquez (D) and Yvette Herrell (R) was rated a Toss-Up by Cook Political Report and was decided by approximately 4–5 points in Herrell's favor. Combined candidate and outside spending exceeded $30 million across all media. Yet on Meta, the only identifiable NM-specific GOTV operation was ProgressNow New Mexico, which spent just $6K–$11K on 44 ads.

ProgressNow NM's ads mixed GOTV messaging ("Hey NM! At nmvote.org, find Early or Election Day voting locations") with anti-Herrell persuasion ("Yvette Herrell says she's not extreme, yet just this past weekend she stood behind the same flag white nationalists..."). At $6K–$11K, this was effectively a rounding error in a $30M+ race.

Where did the NM GOTV money go? The most likely explanation: NM-2 GOTV spending was concentrated on television, direct mail, and ground operations rather than Meta. The Congressional Leadership Fund (R) and House Majority PAC (D) — the primary outside spenders in NM-2 — typically allocate the bulk of their budgets to broadcast and cable TV in smaller media markets where it's cheaper and reaches a higher percentage of the electorate. Southern New Mexico's media markets (Las Cruces, El Paso overlap) are relatively inexpensive for TV, reducing the marginal value of shifting dollars to Meta.

Additionally, NM's long early voting period (October 8 – November 2, nearly 26 days) and same-day voter registration mean that GOTV is less about a single Election Day push and more about sustained engagement — a dynamic better suited to ground operations and mail than digital ad blitzes.

HeadCount's NM presence HeadCount ran at least one NM-specific ad ("Make your voice heard in New Mexico! Your polls are open 7am - 7pm") but at minimal spend ($0–$99). National nonpartisan groups acknowledged NM without investing meaningfully there.

Messaging Strategies

GOTV messaging on Meta fell into distinct patterns that varied sharply between campaigns and outside groups, and between left and right:

GOTV Messaging Framework
How different advertiser types framed their voter mobilization appeals
Campaign GOTV
  • Trump: Urgency + fear of loss. "STOP WHAT YOU'RE DOING. Do you want North Carolina going to Kamala?" State-specific targeting with localized urgency.
  • Allred: Primarily fundraising with GOTV framing. "I'm on the brink of defeating Ted Cruz — chip in $15." The GOTV hook was implicit: donations fund the ground game.
  • Harris: Persuasion-plus-turnout. Issue-focused ads (childcare costs, reproductive rights) paired with "make a plan to vote" calls to action.
Outside Group GOTV
  • Sendthevote: Right-leaning populist. "Open borders. Endless Wars. High Prices. Here's your power move: VOTE!" Culturally resonant ("childless cat ladies" reference).
  • HeadCount: State-specific logistics. "Your polls are open 7am-8pm. Not registered? You can register and vote at your polling place." Pure information delivery.
  • Voto Latino: Registration-focused. High-volume, short-runway campaigns timed to state deadlines. Bilingual content targeting Latino voters.

The most effective messaging pattern, by impression efficiency, was logistical specificity — telling voters exactly when, where, and how to vote. HeadCount's state-by-state "your polls are open" ads generated massive reach on modest budgets. The least efficient pattern was ideological GOTV — ads that spent more time arguing positions than providing voting information. Hard Asset Heroes' $1.3M–$1.5M spend generated relatively fewer impressions, suggesting either narrow targeting or lower engagement rates.

Strategic Takeaway

The Outside Group Asymmetry Is Structural, Not Cyclical

The progressive coalition fielded six or more Meta GOTV operations — civic nonprofits (HeadCount, TurnUp), Latino-focused organizations (Voto Latino), state-specific machines (Powered by People, TX Organizing Project), and national super PACs (Forward Blue). The conservative coalition relied on three: Sendthevote, Hard Asset Heroes, and Turning Point Action. This isn't a one-cycle fluke. It reflects decades of investment in progressive civic infrastructure that has no analogue on the right. The RNC's 2024 decision to outsource GOTV to allied groups may have been an attempt to close this gap, but the Meta data suggests it remains wide.

6 vs. 3 Progressive vs. conservative Meta GOTV operations

Three implications for future cycles:

1. The burst vs. marathon question: Trump's five-day, $3.4M–$4.6M blitz and the progressive coalition's multi-week sustained operations represent genuinely different theories of digital GOTV. The burst model is cheaper to execute (less creative production, less targeting optimization) but risks audience fatigue and ad saturation. The marathon model builds frequency and name recognition but requires sustained investment. Neither approach "won" — both campaigns lost some races they invested in — but the data shows these are conscious strategic choices, not accidents.

2. Texas is a GOTV island: National groups treated Texas as a non-battleground at the presidential level and allocated GOTV dollars elsewhere. This left Texas GOTV entirely to state-specific organizations and the Allred campaign. If Texas ever reaches genuine presidential battleground status, the infrastructure gap between its existing GOTV ecosystem and what national campaigns deploy in swing states (PA, GA, MI) will be enormous.

3. Small states get ignored on Meta: New Mexico's near-absence from Meta GOTV spending despite hosting a nationally competitive House race suggests that Meta is not the platform of choice for GOTV in smaller media markets. TV, mail, and field operations likely absorbed the NM-2 GOTV budget. This has implications for how future races in similar markets plan their digital strategies.

Methodology

Data Source & Limitations

All ad data was retrieved from the Meta Ad Library API on February 10, 2026, covering ads classified as political, social, or issue ads that ran in the United States between August 1 and November 5, 2024.

Search methodology: We identified GOTV advertisers through multiple keyword searches ("vote," "register to vote," "election day," "early voting," "make a plan to vote," "polling location," "ballot") combined with top-spender analysis and page-specific lookups for known GOTV organizations. We then retrieved spend summaries, ad content, geographic delivery, demographic delivery, and weekly volume data for each major advertiser.

Limitations:

  • Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact figures. We use upper-bound estimates for rankings and comparisons, and report ranges where precision matters.
  • Keyword searches may miss GOTV ads that use different terminology, visual-only content, or non-English language.
  • The "campaigns vs. outside groups" boundary is based on the "paid for by" disclaimer. Some entities blur this line.
  • Geographic delivery data reflects who saw ads, not who was targeted. Algorithmic optimization, audience uploads, and lookalike modeling all affect delivery patterns independently of explicit geographic targeting.
  • Demographic percentages from the API represent per-ad averages, not aggregate totals, and appear small in absolute terms.
  • This analysis covers Meta platforms only (Facebook, Instagram, Messenger, Audience Network). It does not capture GOTV spending on Google/YouTube, programmatic display, CTV, direct mail, TV, radio, or field operations.
  • Some advertisers may have run additional ads under different page names or through multiple pages not captured in our tracking.

Classification notes: We classified "Sendthevote" and "Hard Asset Heroes" as right-leaning based on their ad content (references to "open borders," "endless wars," and culturally conservative framing). HeadCount and TurnUp are classified as nonpartisan based on their organizational 501(c)(3) status and content that avoids candidate endorsement. Forward Blue is classified as left-leaning based on its "paid for by" disclaimer and ad content supporting Democratic candidates.
