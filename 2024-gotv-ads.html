The Headline

In Texas, Meta was a fundraising machine — Colin Allred spent $3.3M on Facebook and Instagram, but almost none of it told Texans to go vote. His most-spent ads asked for $15 donations, not early votes. The actual GOTV work on Meta fell to outside groups, particularly Beto O'Rourke's Powered by People ($1.4M on voter registration) and the Texas Organizing Project ($125K on hyper-local down-ballot races). In New Mexico, the $30M NM-2 race barely existed on Meta at all — a combined ~$63K across all advertisers, while America PAC ran the most aggressive digital GOTV operation in the district from across the country. Both races went to Republicans. The data raises hard questions about whether the Democratic Meta strategy in these states was GOTV at all, or just digital fundraising with a GOTV veneer.

Allred's Messaging: The Fundraiser-in-Chief

Colin Allred's Meta operation was overwhelmingly a fundraising machine. His top-frequency words tell the story: Cruz (1,018 mentions), Ted (913), chip (260), grassroots (242), defeat (192), polls (187), tied (173), momentum (161). This is the vocabulary of a donation ask, not a GOTV operation.

His ads fell into three buckets:

1. Poll Momentum → Donate ($$$)

  • "Colin Allred is officially TIED with Ted Cruz"
  • "BREAKING: Texas is Democrats' BEST pickup opportunity"
  • "A new poll JUST came out showing me leading by 1 point"

Highest-spend category. Multiple ads at $5K–$9K each.

2. Character Attack → Donate

  • "In the NFL, we called guys like Ted Cruz 'me guys'"
  • "Flying off to Cancun in the middle of a crisis"
  • "Totally self-serving. All talk, no action."

$7K–$9K on highest-spend versions. NFL framing was distinctive.

3. Urgency/Deadline → Donate

  • "NO REGRETS. Win or lose, I want to wake up on Nov 6"
  • "Ted Cruz is on the ropes — keep our grassroots momentum"
  • "The DSCC is investing in Texas"

Standard campaign fundraising urgency language.

What's Missing

  • No "find your polling place" ads
  • No "vote early" messaging
  • No voter registration deadline reminders
  • No bilingual content

The GOTV hook was implicit at best: donate so we can win.

Cruz's Messaging: Attack and Mobilize

Cruz spent roughly one-quarter of what Allred did on Meta ($865K vs. $3.3M), but his messaging was more GOTV-oriented. His ads split into:

1. "Too Radical for Texas"

  • "Watch Now! Allred is Too Radical for Texas"
  • Video-driven attack ads framing Allred as extreme

Multiple ads at $900–$2K each. Persuasion + mobilization.

2. Direct GOTV

  • "Vote Early in Texas. We need Early Votes!"
  • Single ad, $5K–$6K spend

Rare but present. Cruz at least asked people to vote.

3. Crime + Support

  • "Colin Allred is soft on crime. Support Ted Cruz"
  • Fundraising-oriented but with implied turnout ask

$200–$400 range each. Lower priority than Allred attacks.

Key Difference

  • Cruz ran at least one explicit "vote early" ad
  • Allred ran zero
  • Cruz's fundraising asks were simpler: "Did you see this? Support Ted Cruz"
The Allred Meta paradox Allred outspent Cruz on Meta nearly 4-to-1. But his spend was almost entirely optimized for out-of-state small-dollar donations, not Texas voter turnout. His top-performing ad ("Did you know that right now, Colin is in the fight of his life to defeat Ted Cruz?") ran at $8K–$9K in multiple versions — it's a fundraising pitch delivered to national Democratic donors, not a message to Texas voters. Allred's actual GOTV operation — field offices, canvassers, phone banks — was funded by these donations but happened off Meta.

Texas Outside Groups: Where the Real GOTV Happened

If Allred's Meta operation was a fundraising engine, the outside groups were the actual GOTV apparatus. Three organizations filled the gap:

Texas-Focused Outside Group Spending
Groups with significant Texas GOTV operations on Meta

Powered by People (Beto O'Rourke) — $1.1M–$1.4M

The largest dedicated Texas GOTV operation on Meta. Beto's organization ran 1,438 ads, overwhelmingly video content with no text. The messaging themes from the few text-bearing ads reveal the strategy:

"For most of this year we have been laser focused on registering first time Democratic voters. As part of that effort, I've traveled to college campuses to speak and connect with young college voters…" — Beto O'Rourke / Powered by People

Top keywords: voters (902), Texas (862), turn (443), election (381), reach (379), voter (335), college (229), registered (198), nonvoting (186), mobilize (184). This is a registration-and-turnout vocabulary. Powered by People was doing what Allred's campaign was not: telling Texans to register and vote.

College campus strategy Powered by People explicitly targeted first-time voters at Texas universities. The word "college" appeared 229 times in their ad text. Combined with the video-heavy format (most ads had "no text content," suggesting video creative), this was a youth-registration operation delivered through the platform young voters actually use: Instagram.

Texas Organizing Project — $89K–$125K

TOP ran the most granular GOTV operation in our dataset. Their 193 ads ignored statewide races entirely, focusing on county-level candidates and ballot measures in Harris County (Houston), Dallas County, and Bexar County (San Antonio).

"Let's elect champions including Sean Teare, Annette Ramirez, Christian Menefee, and Ed Gonzalez who will fight for a future where ALL Harris County residents can thrive." — Texas Organizing Project PAC
"HISD's state-appointed superintendent and school board cannot be trusted with a $4.4 billion bond. Vote AGAINST Propositions A and B in HISD's bond election." — Texas Organizing Project PAC

Top keywords: Texas (142), race (81), vote (80), progressive (50), leaders (50), rights (44), gender (41), county (37), ballot (36), HISD (28). This is the "below the fold" GOTV that national coverage misses: county judges, school bonds, propositions. TOP's theory is that voters who turn out for local races they care about also vote up-ballot.

Voto Latino — $1.1M–$1.7M (multi-state, TX portion estimated ~$200K)

Voto Latino ran a national operation across swing states plus Texas. Their signature move was a massive spending spike ($699K–$1M) in the week of September 30, timed precisely to the Texas voter registration deadline (October 7). Texas was one of several target states, and their ads focused on registration logistics rather than candidate endorsement.

This is the most striking finding in our analysis. NM-2 was a Cook Political Report Toss-Up — one of the most competitive House races in America. Combined spending across all media exceeded $30 million. Herrell won by approximately 4–5 points, flipping the seat.

Yet on Meta, the entire NM-2 universe amounted to roughly $63K in total ad spend across all advertisers. The Vasquez campaign itself appears to have run zero Meta ads under its FEC-registered disclaimer.

NM-2 Messaging: Two Completely Different Campaigns

The NM-2 Meta advertising split into two distinct messaging worlds with almost no overlap:

Pro-Herrell Side: National Templates

Three themes dominated, all driven by national organizations rather than the Herrell campaign itself:

Border/Immigration (dominant): America PAC ran the most aggressive messaging — "Vasquez called the border wall 'racist,'" "tired of NM being taken over by criminals," "secure our border." This was a national template adapted with NM-2 specifics.

Energy/Economy: AFP Action framed Herrell as pro-energy bipartisan: "end Washington's out-of-control spending and unleash American energy." State legislator David Gallegos warned Vasquez would "eliminate every job in Southeastern New Mexico."

Trump Alignment: Herrell's own ads and Women for America's Freedom explicitly tied her to Trump: "President Trump and Yvette Herrell will secure the southern border."

Pro-Vasquez Side: Local & Community

The pro-Vasquez ecosystem was diffuse and locally focused:

Working Families (OLÉ): Bilingual ads about wages, childcare, Medicaid, paid sick leave. "New Mexico Democrats have been fighting for working families." State legislature candidates, not Vasquez directly.

Reproductive Rights (OLÉ): "Mary Ingham's extremist views on women's healthcare are a threat to our fundamental rights." Targeted at NM state House races, not the federal race.

Simple ID (Blue CD2): "Vote Gabe Vasquez" repeated with no elaboration. Video-driven. Name recognition play.

America PAC: The Most Innovative GOTV Tactic in the Dataset

Elon Musk's America PAC ($4M–$5.2M nationally, 2,351 ads) ran a multi-state GOTV operation that included NM-2. Their most distinctive tactic was social pressure GOTV:

"Your neighbors are voting. Your family is voting. Your voting record is public — They'll know if you don't join them. Do not be left out. GET OUT AND VOTE EARLY TODAY!" — America PAC

This "your neighbors are voting" approach draws on well-documented social pressure research showing that informing voters their participation is tracked by their community increases turnout by 2–8 percentage points. It's been used in direct mail for years but was relatively novel as a Meta ad strategy at this scale. America PAC paired it with state-specific attack content (border messaging in NM, anti-Harris in swing states).

Their NM-2 specific ads were pure attack-plus-GOTV: frame Vasquez as pro-open-borders, then tell people to vote Herrell. Clean, simple, aggressive.

05

OLÉ New Mexico: The Bilingual Ground Game

Organizers in the Land of Enchantment was the most substantive progressive Meta advertiser in New Mexico. Their 180 ads and $13.5K–$31.3K spend was modest in absolute terms, but their approach was distinctive — and worth studying.

OLÉ ran fully bilingual campaigns. Many ads had Spanish-language versions running alongside English, targeting NM's ~49% Hispanic population:

"Charlotte Little luchará para que nuestras familias trabajadoras conserven el dinero que ganamos tomando medidas contra los aumentos abusivos de precios…" — OLÉ NM (Spanish-language ad)
"It's time to break the cycle of poverty wages for indigenous Early Educators in McKinley County!" — OLÉ NM (targeting Native American communities)

Their ads covered state legislature candidates (Charlotte Little, Frank Baca, Sarah Silva), issue framing (housing, education, childcare, price gouging), opposition research (Ali Ennenga, Mary Ingham), and Vasquez endorsement. The Vasquez ad was notable for being the only outside-group ad we found that framed Vasquez positively on policy:

"Congressman Gabe Vasquez is a champion for working families… he's working tirelessly to protect and expand programs like Medicaid and the Affordable Care Act." — OLÉ NM
OLÉ's theory of the case OLÉ didn't try to compete with the $30M being spent on TV for NM-2. Instead, they used Meta to mobilize specific communities — Spanish-speaking families, indigenous educators, progressive state-legislature voters — who would also vote for Vasquez up-ballot. This "build from the bottom" approach is the opposite of the national groups' top-down model. At $31K maximum spend, it was the best-targeted dollar-for-dollar investment in the NM dataset.

Both Allred and Vasquez lost. That complicates any claim about "what worked." But we can assess effectiveness on narrower terms: which approaches generated the most reach per dollar, which filled genuine GOTV gaps, and which represented strategic coherence.

The Effectiveness Lesson

The organizations that generated the most value on Meta weren't the ones that spent the most. They were the ones that did something nobody else was doing. Powered by People ran the only large-scale Texas registration operation. TX Organizing Project was the only entity running county-level GOTV. OLÉ was the only bilingual NM operation. AFP Action was the only voice framing Herrell as bipartisan. When Meta dollars filled a unique gap in the broader campaign ecosystem, they worked. When they duplicated what TV, mail, and field were already doing — or when they were just fundraising — they were noise.

Methodology

Data Source & Limitations

All ad data was retrieved from the Meta Ad Library API on February 10, 2026. The analysis covers ads classified as political, social, or issue ads that ran in the United States between August 1 and November 5, 2024.

Search methodology: We identified TX and NM GOTV advertisers through keyword searches (candidate names, "vote," "register," "early voting," "election day," "border," "Texas," "New Mexico"), page searches for known organizations, and top-spender analysis. We then retrieved spend summaries, ad content, messaging theme analysis, and geographic delivery data for each advertiser.

Scope: This report focuses on non-presidential campaigns and outside groups active in Texas and New Mexico. Presidential campaign spending (Trump, Harris, Biden) is excluded. The "campaigns" category includes official candidate committees (Allred, Cruz, Herrell, Vasquez). Everything else is classified as "outside groups."

Limitations: Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact amounts. Keyword searches may miss ads using different terminology or visual-only content. The Vasquez campaign's apparent absence from Meta may reflect ads run under a different page name or disclaimer not captured by our searches. Impression-per-dollar calculations use midpoint impressions and upper-bound spend, creating rough estimates rather than precise metrics. Geographic delivery data reflects who saw ads, not targeting intent. This analysis covers Meta platforms only and does not capture TV, mail, digital display, or field operations which likely represented the bulk of spending in both races.
