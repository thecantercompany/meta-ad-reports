Who Is the Permian Strategic Partnership?

The Permian Strategic Partnership (PSP) is a coalition of 27 leading Permian Basin energy companies and two university systems, formed in 2019 to address quality-of-life challenges in the oil-producing region spanning 22 counties across West Texas and Southeastern New Mexico.1 Since its founding, PSP has committed $184 million in philanthropic investments that it says have leveraged over $1.8 billion in total community support.2

PSP focuses on four areas: education ($80.1M invested), healthcare ($63.4M), workforce development ($25.8M), and road safety ($13.7M). In 2024 alone, PSP committed nearly $31 million to these initiatives.2

The coalition represents some of the largest oil and gas producers operating in the Permian Basin, the most productive oil field in the United States. Ten counties in the Permian account for 93% of all U.S. oil production growth since 2020.

Strategic Context PSP's community investment model is common among extractive industry coalitions: member companies pool philanthropic dollars to address community needs that arise from rapid resource development — housing shortages, strained schools, overwhelmed healthcare systems, dangerous roads. The advertising examined in this report represents a tiny fraction of PSP's overall budget, but it reveals which issues the organization chose to amplify publicly on social media and when.

Campaign Deep Dives

The Permian Counts: 2020 Census Mobilization

PSP's largest Meta advertising investment was its 2020 Census campaign, run through a dedicated Facebook Page called "The Permian Counts."8 The campaign launched on March 16, 2020 — just days after COVID-19 was declared a national emergency — and ran through April 21. In approximately five weeks, PSP deployed 81 ads spending $46,000–$60,000 and generating an estimated 5.3–6.3 million impressions.

The urgency was real. A study by The Perryman Group, commissioned by PSP, estimated that census undercounts in the Permian could result in losses of $1.1 billion in gross product and 13,135 job-years of employment over the following decade.3 The Permian Basin's transient workforce population — workers who move in and out with drilling activity — made undercounts especially likely. For an industry whose federal and state funding allocations, political representation, and infrastructure investment depend on population counts, maximizing census participation was a direct business interest as much as a civic one.

Campaign Scale 81 ads in ~5 weeks is an aggressive deployment for a single-issue regional campaign. The volume suggests PSP was A/B testing extensively across demographics and geographies — running parallel ad variants in Texas (targeting Austin as the state capital) and New Mexico (targeting Santa Fe) with identical messaging but localized calls to action.

The messaging operated on two parallel tracks:

If you're not counted, you're hurting the Permian community. Millions in funding and thousands of jobs are at risk.

~42 ads · Loss-aversion framing · View sample ad

Let Austin and Washington, D.C. know the Permian counts! It means more jobs and more money for our community.

~39 ads · Empowerment framing · View sample ad

The first message used loss aversion — you're actively hurting your community by not participating. The second framed the census as empowerment — a chance to make Permian voices heard in Austin and D.C. Both messages centered jobs and money, the two themes most likely to resonate in a region where the economy revolves around energy production.

New Mexico-targeted ads swapped "Austin" for "Santa Fe" but were otherwise identical, reflecting the Permian Basin's cross-state geography (Lea and Eddy counties in NM are as central to the basin as Midland and Martin counties in TX).

Voter Registration: 2022 Midterms

A small, low-budget voter registration push ran on the Permian Strategic Partnership page from September 23 to October 10, 2022 — the Texas voter registration deadline for the November midterms. Seven ads spent less than $700 combined, generating roughly 25,000 impressions.

Unlike the census campaign, these ads were explicitly nonpartisan — simple reminders of registration deadlines with links to the Texas Secretary of State's registration portal. Notably, the geographic delivery data shows these ads reached New Mexico exclusively, despite referencing Texas voter registration. This likely reflects the Permian Basin's cross-border audience: PSP's page followers include NM residents, and Meta's delivery algorithm may have found higher engagement rates there.

Education Advocacy: 2025 "Future Ready" Campaign

PSP's most recent advertising push, launched in March 2025 and still active, marks a strategic pivot from civic mobilization to education advocacy. Running 25 ads spending $4,000–$7,500 so far, the campaign drives traffic to permianbasinfutureready.com and urges parents and community members to demand higher education standards from local school districts.

The messaging is more pointed than the census campaign. Where the 2020 effort asked people to participate in a federal process, the 2025 ads challenge the status quo with a direct question:

Is your child getting the education they deserve? Students deserve a high quality education that unlocks their potential and empowers them to succeed. Yet, the Permian Basin is missing out on key high-quality education opportunities that exist in other parts of the state. Less than half of students 3rd–8th grade read at grade level while only one third meet math standards.

Why Education, Why Now? Education quality is the #1 factor PSP member companies cite when evaluating whether to relocate employees to the Permian Basin.4 In 2025, PSP launched a $10 million initiative to establish a third Harmony Public School campus in the region,5 supported Pathway Academy's opening in Big Spring (serving grades 7–12),6 and invested in career technical education centers.7 The Facebook ads are the public-facing tip of a much larger education reform effort. The "Future Ready" campaign appears designed to generate community pressure on school districts to adopt higher standards — a classic grasstops mobilization strategy where industry backs parent advocacy.

Messaging Themes

Word Frequency by Campaign

Top keywords from ad text across both pages

The two campaigns occupy distinct semantic territories. The census campaign anchored every message around community, jobs, money, and funding — the economic consequences of being undercounted. The education campaign centers schools, standards, families, and futures — aspirational language about what the Permian could become.

What both campaigns share is a consistent avoidance of oil-and-gas industry framing. Neither campaign mentions energy, oil, drilling, or any individual member company. The messaging presents PSP purely as a community organization concerned with civic participation and children's education. This is deliberate brand positioning: the ads build social license by associating the coalition with universally popular causes rather than industry-specific advocacy.

Both campaigns delivered overwhelmingly to Texas and New Mexico — the two states that contain the Permian Basin — with minimal spillover. This tight geographic concentration is consistent with hyper-local targeting, likely using radius targeting around Permian Basin cities (Midland, Odessa, Carlsbad, Hobbs, Pecos).

The age distribution tells a more nuanced story. The census campaign reached a broad age range with a peak in 25–44 year olds — the demographic most likely to be undercounted in a transient workforce region. The education campaign skews slightly younger, peaking in 25–34, consistent with targeting parents of school-age children. Gender delivery was roughly balanced across both campaigns, with the census campaign skewing slightly toward women (60/40).

Strategic Takeaway

The Permian Strategic Partnership's Meta advertising reveals a disciplined, issue-specific approach rather than a continuous brand presence. PSP advertises only when there is a concrete civic objective — census participation, voter registration, education reform — and goes silent in between. This is the opposite of an always-on corporate reputation campaign.

The pattern also reveals strategic escalation. The 2020 census campaign was the big spend — $46K–$60K to ensure the Permian Basin was properly counted, protecting federal funding and political representation that directly benefit the energy industry and its workforce. The 2025 education campaign is smaller in ad dollars but potentially higher in strategic ambition: PSP is now actively mobilizing community members to pressure school districts on education standards, a grasstops organizing technique that extends the coalition's influence from philanthropy into policy advocacy.

What PSP doesn't advertise is as telling as what it does. There are no ads defending oil and gas, no climate messaging, no regulatory pushback — all common themes from industry trade groups like API or Energy Citizens. PSP's advertising identity is entirely civic: census, voting, schools. This positioning allows the coalition to build community goodwill and social license without triggering the adversarial dynamics that accompany explicitly pro-fossil-fuel messaging.

Methodology & Data Notes

This report examines all political, social, and issue ads run by the Permian Strategic Partnership across two Facebook Pages:

  • Permian Strategic Partnership (Page ID: 101245049102141) — 32 ads, $4,600–$8,168 spend
  • The Permian Counts (Page ID: 101315098149432) — 81 ads, $46,000–$59,719 spend

Data was retrieved from the Meta Ad Library API on February 10, 2025. Key limitations:

  • Meta reports spend as ranges, not exact amounts. All spend figures in this report reflect these ranges.
  • Demographic and geographic data reflects delivery (who saw the ads), not targeting (who the advertiser intended to reach). Delivery skews can be caused by audience lists, algorithmic optimization, creative resonance, or platform effects — not just targeting settings.
  • The Meta Ad Library only captures ads classified as political, social, or issue ads. PSP may run additional commercial or non-political ads that do not appear in this dataset.
  • Impression counts are estimates provided by Meta and should be treated as approximations.
