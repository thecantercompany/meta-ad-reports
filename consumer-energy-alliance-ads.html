Strategic Takeaway

Consumer Energy Alliance—a 501(c)(4) operated by Houston PR firm HBW Resources on behalf of the American Petroleum Institute and major oil companies—runs precision-targeted Meta ad campaigns designed to look like grassroots consumer advocacy. Its $258K–$395K in Meta political ad spending across 968 ads serves a single strategic purpose: manufacturing the appearance of public support for fossil fuel infrastructure while opposing clean energy regulations. The organization has been repeatedly caught submitting fraudulent public comments using real people's identities—including a 9-year-old child—making its Facebook ad operations part of a broader pattern of astroturf tactics.

Organization Profile

Who Is Consumer Energy Alliance?

Despite branding itself as "the voice of the energy consumer," CEA is an industry-funded advocacy operation designed to give fossil fuel interests the appearance of broad-based public support.

Founded
2006, with founding board members from Shell and Hess Corporation. Co-founded by Michael Whatley (now RNC Chair) with backing from BP, Chevron, ExxonMobil, Marathon, Shell, and Statoil.
Tax Status
501(c)(4) civic league / social welfare organization. Not required to disclose donors.
Operated By
HBW Resources, a Houston-based PR and lobbying firm headed by David Holt and Andrew Browning. CEA has allocated $9.1 million (43% of revenue) to HBW since 2010.
Revenue
$21.1 million+ since 2010. Regular funding from the American Petroleum Institute and American Natural Gas Alliance (2008–2012 documented).
President
David Holt (since 2006), who simultaneously serves as Managing Partner of HBW Resources—the firm paid to run CEA.
Lobbying
$90,000 spent on federal lobbying in 2025 YTD. $200,000 in 2022. Advocates for offshore drilling, pipeline projects, refinery expansion, and permitting acceleration.

Documented Fraud & Astroturfing

Wisconsin (2014): Submitted a fraudulent petition attacking net metering with 2,500 names of utility customers who didn't authorize it.

Ohio (2016): Sent 347 letters to FERC supporting a Nexus Gas pipeline using names of local residents—including a man who had been dead since 1998.

South Carolina (2018): Sent fraudulent emails to state legislators bearing names and addresses of residents who said they were impersonated.

Ohio (2023): Over 1,000 pro-fracking public comments traced to CEA; 137 people said they didn't knowingly agree. One letter bore the name of a 9-year-old child. The Washington Post reported the comments stemmed from "misleading" Facebook ads. Ohio AG Dave Yost issued subpoenas.

State-Level Campaign Analysis

CEA's State-by-State Operations

CEA runs geographically precise campaigns, geo-targeting ads to individual states where energy policy is being debated. Each campaign follows a consistent playbook: frame fossil fuel interests as consumer protection, cite proprietary cost estimates for energy transitions, and mobilize public comments through petition-style call-to-action links.

Five Messaging Strategies

1. Cost-Shock Framing

The most aggressive tactic. CEA publishes proprietary "reports" claiming energy transitions will cost households $26,000–$102,000, then runs ads citing those figures as authoritative. The reports are produced by or for HBW Resources—the same firm that operates CEA—creating a self-referential credibility loop. These figures are not independently verified and are significantly higher than peer estimates.

2. Small-Town Storytelling

CEA profiles real local businesses—breweries, chile producers, landscaping companies—to humanize fossil fuel dependence. The Michigan Line 5 campaign featured women-owned businesses and generational farming families. The New Mexico campaign profiles local restaurants and tourism businesses. This is the most sophisticated tactic: real people providing authentic emotional resonance for industry-authored messaging.

3. Legislative Mobilization

During active legislative fights, CEA shifts to direct call-to-action ads urging constituents to contact legislators. The Colorado HB-1294 campaign and the D.C. gas price campaign both used "ACT NOW" language with petition-style sign-up forms. This tactic has been directly linked to the fraudulent public comment incidents in Ohio, Wisconsin, and South Carolina.

4. Populist Anti-Government

The Connecticut PURA campaign exemplifies this approach: attacking government agencies for rate increases while omitting that the utility companies CEA's funders represent are the primary beneficiaries of deregulation. Language like "#CTPowerGrab" and "#StopTheSubsidy" positions clean energy investment as government overreach.

5. Patriotic Energy Independence

The January 2026 ads deploy national security framing: "American energy made here at home." This messaging intensifies around federal policy shifts and is particularly concentrated in Texas, where it serves as base-building for CEA's corporate members. The link title "America Can't Afford a 1970s Playbook for 2025 Energy Needs" positions clean energy regulation as outdated thinking.

Tactical Analysis

How CEA Uses Meta Ads

A/B Testing at Scale

CEA routinely deploys 10–50 ad variants with identical or near-identical text but different creative assets. The Virginia gas ban campaign ran 20+ variants of the same "$26,132" message. The Colorado "$1,100 savings" campaign ran 10+ identical text variants. This is a systematic A/B testing operation—not casual posting—indicating professional media buying with performance optimization.

Low-Spend, High-Volume Deployment

The vast majority of individual ads spend under $100 each. Only 14% exceed $100, and fewer than 3% exceed $1,000. CEA's strategy is many small bets rather than a few large placements, allowing rapid testing and optimization before concentrating spend on winners.

Facebook + Instagram Only

All ads run on Facebook and Instagram. No evidence of Audience Network or other Meta placement expansion, suggesting a deliberate strategy to reach engaged users in news feed environments where the content mimics organic posts.

Demographics Reached

The demographic breakdown shows a roughly 50/50 male-female split across the overall portfolio, though individual campaigns skew significantly. The Michigan Line 5 campaign over-indexed on 55–65+ audiences (core homeowner demographics), while the Connecticut PURA campaign reached a younger 18–34 audience. The Colorado HB-1294 campaign skewed dramatically older: 69% of reach went to 55+ audiences.

Cross-Platform Context

Beyond Meta: CEA's Broader Operations

Meta ads are one channel in a multi-pronged influence operation. CEA's broader toolkit includes:

Federal Lobbying: $90K in 2025, $200K in 2022. Lobbying targets include offshore drilling, pipeline permitting, and LNG export approvals. The D.C.-targeted Facebook ads run simultaneously with lobbying activity, creating a pincer strategy: lobby officials directly while manufacturing constituent pressure through ads.

Proprietary Reports: CEA publishes state-specific "cost reports" on energy transitions, then cites them in ads. Reports are produced by HBW Resources, creating a closed loop of self-referencing credibility.

Public Comment Campaigns: Facebook ads drive users to petition-style forms. CEA has been caught converting these sign-ups into public comments to regulatory bodies without adequate informed consent—and in some cases, fabricating comments entirely.

Regional Chapters: CEA Mid-Atlantic, CEA Colorado, and other regional brands give a local appearance to the Houston-based operation.

Methodology & Data Limitations

This report analyzes Consumer Energy Alliance's political and issue ad activity on Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram) from the Meta Ad Library, which archives ads classified as political, electoral, or issue-related. Data was retrieved on February 5, 2026.

  • Spend figures are ranges, not exact amounts. Meta reports spend in brackets (e.g., "$1,000–$1,499"). Totals represent the sum of upper-bound estimates across all tracked periods.
  • Ad count (968) includes all variants. Many "unique" ads share identical or near-identical text with different creative assets, inflating the raw count. Distinct messaging campaigns number approximately 15–20.
  • Demographic data reflects delivery, not targeting. The age and gender breakdowns show who Meta's algorithm served the ads to, which may differ from the advertiser's explicit targeting settings.
  • Keyword searches may miss ads. Ads that don't reference energy, gas, or related terms in their text may not appear in keyword-based searches. This report uses page-based retrieval for completeness.
  • Background research draws from public records, including IRS 990 filings, OpenSecrets lobbying data, investigative reporting from the Washington Post, Nexus Media News, Energy and Policy Institute, and DeSmog, and CEA's own website and press releases.
